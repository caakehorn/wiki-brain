# Say Anything

Los Angeles-via-Brooklyn project built almost entirely around frontman
Max Bemis, whose defining record — *...Is a Real Boy* (2004) — is a
confessional concept album written and recorded in the aftermath of a
psychiatric hospitalization, with lyrics that turn self-loathing,
grandiosity, and narcissism into the explicit subject matter rather than
subtext. Bemis doesn't perform vulnerability the way most emo-era
songwriters do — obliquely, through imagery — he names his own worst
traits directly and dares the listener to still like him.

## In Favorites

Six tracks, all logged under the manually curated "MUSIC LIST
(start-2024)": "I Used To Have A Heart," "Walk Through Hell," "Skinny,
Mean Man," "Hate Everyone," "Woe," and "Shiksa (Girlfriend)." Read as a
set, before even considering individual lyrics, the titles alone chart a
consistent arc — the loss of feeling, self-directed cruelty, misanthropy,
grief — that matches Bemis's signature move: writing about his own
defects with clinical precision rather than either hiding them or
performing remorse for them.

> speculation, not a claim about what happens in the moment of listening.

## Why this artist fits

Say Anything's whole appeal rests on a songwriter willing to be the
villain of his own record — to state, flatly, that he is capable of
cruelty, that he sometimes hates people, that he has run out of feeling —
without asking to be forgiven for it inside the song. That's a
meaningfully different move than the mourning-and-devastation register of
[[wiki/interests/favorites/music/artists/my-chemical-romance]] or the
betrayal-narrative structure of
[[wiki/interests/favorites/music/artists/taking-back-sunday]]: Say
Anything isn't singing about being wronged, it's singing about being the
one doing wrong and refusing to soften the self-portrait. For a profile
that elsewhere documents shame being "transmuted into intellectual
weaponry" rather than avoided ([[wiki/mind/profile/deviance-mapping]]),
an artist whose entire career is built on the same move — naming the
flaw precisely instead of concealing it, then using that precision as the
art itself — is a plausible and specific point of identification, not
just genre-adjacent taste.
