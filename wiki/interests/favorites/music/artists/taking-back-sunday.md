# Taking Back Sunday

Long Island band whose 2002 debut, *Tell All Your Friends*, is one of the
genre's most famous real-life betrayal records: original guitarist John
Nolan began a relationship with frontman Adam Lazzara's girlfriend during
the band's early days, and the album — written and recorded while all
three people were still in the same band — processes that specific
betrayal in close to real time. "Cute Without The 'E' (Cut From The
Team)" is the most explicit of these, understood by fans and the band
itself as a direct address to Nolan. This isn't a genre performing
heartbreak as a pose; it's a document of an actual friend-and-bandmate
betrayal, released while the wound was still open.

## In Favorites

Six tracks, five of them from *Tell All Your Friends*: "This Photograph
Is Proof (I Know You Know)," "You're So Last Summer," "Cute Without The
'E' (Cut From The Team)," "Set Phasers To Stun," and "Liar (It Takes One
To Know One)" — that is, the album's betrayal narrative essentially
end to end, not a sampling of its more radio-friendly moments. The
sixth, "Error Operator," is a track from the band's 2016 self-titled
reunion record, showing the engagement continued well past the moment
the debut would have first been discovered.

> **Caveat:** Dan does not process sung lyrics as language while
> listening — words register as timbre, not semantic content (see
> [[wiki/interests/music/concepts/lyrics-as-timbre]]). Knowledge of the
> Nolan/Lazzara betrayal is near-certainly contextual (it's one of the
> genre's most famous backstories) rather than reconstructed from parsing
> the lyrics during playback — this doesn't undercut the track-selection
> pattern below, but it does mean the connection runs through knowing the
> story, not hearing it sung.

## Why this record specifically

Selecting nearly the entirety of *Tell All Your Friends* — a record whose
entire premise is a documented, named, real betrayal by someone who was
supposed to be an ally — lines up directly with the betrayal-and-loyalty
axis that runs through the rest of the self-model: the vertical/lateral
trust asymmetry ([[wiki/mind/synthesis/vertical-authority-skepticism]]),
the attachment architecture that treats closeness as a precursor to loss
([[wiki/mind/concepts/attachment-model]]), and the corpus's own
documented betrayal events (the [[wiki/people/eli|Eli]] incident chief
among them). Taking Back Sunday's album isn't generically sad the way
some emo records are — it's specifically about the exact category of
harm (a trusted person crossing the one line that mattered) that recurs
as the sharpest, most destabilizing category of injury throughout Dan's
own documented history. That's a much more precise point of resonance
than "sad breakup music," and the track selection (betrayal songs, not
the poppier back half of the record) suggests the precision was
intentional rather than incidental.
