# Elliott Smith

Portland singer-songwriter, 1969–2003. Recorded on four-track cassette recorders and later in professional studios, but with a production sensibility that always kept the room quiet enough to hear the breath. Known for *Either/Or* (1997), *XO* (1998), and *Figure 8* (2000) — albums of extraordinary melodic precision built around the kind of personal devastation that sounds like it was written in the dark and shouldn't be overheard.

He died of two stab wounds to the chest in 2003. The circumstances remain disputed.

7 tracks.

## Why This Artist

Smith is the emotional pole of a listening profile that also includes JPEGMAFIA and My Chemical Romance — the quietest, most intimate presence in a library that elsewhere tends toward intensity. The 7 tracks aren't background listening; they're the kind of tracks you put on when the volume of everything else gets too high.

The 2013 Facebook username reference — "patron saint of liars and fakes" — is lifted from Smith's own self-description and reveals something specific about what his music meant at that moment. Smith wrote about self-deception, about the gap between who you present to the world and the thing underneath it, with an exactitude that felt diagnostic rather than confessional. The attraction is not to his sadness as sadness but to the precision with which he named things that were hard to name.

For an INTP 5w4 whose default mode is forensic analysis, the appeal of music this emotionally exact is not really surprising — it's the same mode applied to a different domain. Smith doesn't vague-gesture at feeling; he finds the specific image, the specific chord movement, the specific vocal break that carries the exact weight of the thing. *Between the Bars*, *Waltz #2*, *King's Crossing* — these are not generally sad. They are accurately sad, about specific things, in ways that resist sentimentality.

## The Quiet Anchor

In the context of the broader favorites map — JPEGMAFIA's abrasion, MCR's theatrical volume, the emo cluster's collective emotional intensity — Elliott Smith functions as the proof that the emotional range extends all the way in the other direction. The library isn't just loud; it can hold its breath.
