# Music Favorites

The FAVS MASTERLIST logs 1,860 tracks across 1,477 unique artists — an
average of 1.26 tracks per artist, which is the number that matters more
than any individual count: this is a library built for breadth, not
repeat-loyalty to a small set of favorites. None of it carries a numeric
rating (music entries are inclusion-only, mostly pulled from Spotify
likes, unlike the rated books and art elsewhere in the favorites domain),
so the signal here is presence, not preference intensity — what made the
list at all, not how many stars it got.

**Before reading anything below as "why these lyrics resonate":** Dan
does not process sung lyrics as language while listening — words register
as timbre, not semantic content. See
[[wiki/interests/music/concepts/lyrics-as-timbre]] for the full statement
and what it changes. Every interpretive claim on this page and its
sub-pages about thematic or lyrical content should be read as either (a)
contextual knowledge of what a record is *about*, or (b) speculation
about what the vocal *performance* — texture, strain, delivery — might be
doing, not a claim about real-time lyric comprehension.

## The two ingestion waves, and what they mean

The library isn't one continuous accumulation — it's built from two
distinct sources with different characters. "MUSIC LIST (start-2024)" is
a manually curated list: the entire emo/pop-punk cluster (Fall Out Boy,
My Chemical Romance, Say Anything, Taking Back Sunday) comes from here,
meaning someone sat down and specifically chose these tracks rather than
letting an algorithm surface them — a deliberate act of archiving a
decade-old attachment, not passive nostalgia. "SPOTIFY LIKED 2025-2026"
is the live feed: nearly all of the electronic/bass cluster (LYNY,
Knock2, and the rest of
[[wiki/interests/favorites/music/electronic-bass]]) comes from here,
current and ongoing rather than retrospective. The release-year
distribution confirms the split is real, not incidental: 2025 alone
accounts for 530 tracks and 2026 another 197 — well over a third of the
entire library concentrated in the last eighteen months, against 88 for
2023 and 51 for 2022.

That recent surge lines up with the music-reactivation arc already
documented in [[wiki/timeline/periods/2025-collapse]] and
[[wiki/timeline/periods/dec-2025-spike]] and the return to Pennsylvania —
the library's shape is itself evidence of the same production
reactivation the [[wiki/interests/music/aliases/gripnotic|GRIPNOTIC]] page
tracks independently. Two separate signals (what got listened to, what
got produced) point at the same recovery window.

## Three clusters, one long tail

The top-count artists split cleanly into three roughly even clusters —
experimental/abrasive hip-hop, emo/pop-punk/indie rock, and
electronic/bass — each accounting for something close to 5% of the
library, with a long tail of one- and two-track artists making up most of
the rest. That balance is the tell: this isn't three genres competing for
dominance, it's three genres being maintained in parallel, each with its
own dedicated sub-pages and its own function.

**Experimental/abrasive hip-hop** (JPEGMAFIA, Kanye West, and a longer
tail including A$AP Rocky, Lil Wayne, Tyler the Creator, Atmosphere,
Lupe Fiasco) is dense, confrontational, self-produced-adjacent work — see
[[wiki/interests/favorites/music/artists/jpegmafia]] for the fullest
treatment of why this cluster in particular maps onto the production
identity.

**Emo, pop-punk, and indie rock** (My Chemical Romance, New Found Glory,
Fall Out Boy, Taking Back Sunday, Say Anything, Elliott Smith, plus Bloc
Party, Death Cab for Cutie, Manchester Orchestra, The Mars Volta) is the
manually-archived, decade-old cluster — five artist pages now cover it in
detail, and the pattern across them is consistent: concept albums and
whole-catalog engagement built around betrayal, mortality, and
self-indictment, timed to a genre and era (2005–2010) that overlaps
directly with the "romantic collapse fantasy" imprinting window named
elsewhere in the profile ([[wiki/mind/psychosexual/developmental-origins]]).

**Electronic, bass, and modern dance** (LYNY, Knock2, Effin, Mau P,
PEEKABOO, Levity, Nikita the Wicked, rSUN, oskar med k, borne, A.M.C) is
the live, current-listening cluster and the one with the most direct tie
to Dan's own work — see [[wiki/interests/favorites/music/electronic-bass]]
for the full treatment, including the case that this cluster functions as
active calibration listening for the GRIPNOTIC-era production practice,
not just consumption running in parallel to it.

## Continuity with the 2007–2014 Facebook record

The current library isn't a break from earlier taste — it's a direct
continuation of it. The Facebook archive ([[wiki/self/facebook]],
2007–2022) shows the same artists surfacing more than a decade earlier:
Elliott Smith liked in August 2013 (he has 7 tracks in the current
library), Fall Out Boy and Say Anything both name-checked in 2007 status
updates ("currently swooning over the new FOB," "in love with the new Say
Anything disc"), Radiohead on loop the same year. The electronic side has
an equally long tail: 2012–2014 likes include ODESZA, JAUZ, Borgore,
Flying Lotus, Diplo, and Zedd, and event/photo data places Dan at Electric
Zoo in 2010 and a run of raves and DJ shows from 2014–2019 (High Tide,
Freddie Gibbs, United Ravers, Space Jesus). The emo attachment and the
bass attachment are both at least eighteen years old by the time the 2024
manual list and the 2025–2026 Spotify feed pick them back up — this
library isn't discovering a taste, it's the latest layer of one that's
been continuous since adolescence.

## Cultural framing: the rock-irrelevance thesis

Both major clusters here (hip-hop and electronic/bass) sit on the winning
side of an explicit argument Dan has made about the current cultural
moment: that guitar-driven rock lost its dominant position after 1963 and
has never gotten it back, backed with streaming, market-share, and
instrument-sales data. See [[wiki/interests/rock-irrelevance-thesis]] for
the full case. The point isn't nostalgia dressed as contrarianism — it's
the opposite claim: that the electronic and hip-hop concentration here
represents alignment with where the actual cultural center of gravity
sits, not a retreat into a niche.
