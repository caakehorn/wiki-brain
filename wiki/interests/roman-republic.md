# Roman Republic & Ancient History

The books favorites break down flagged ancient history as an
"interest in foundational power dynamics" — one line
([[wiki/interests/favorites/books/topics/ancient-history]]). The reality
is bigger: **2024 was a full-immersion ancient-history year** for Dan, a
concentrated, evangelical binge on the fall of the Roman Republic and the
"great man" biography, documented both in the reading dates and in the
messages where he tries to hand it to everyone around him.

## The 2024 reading binge — dated, not vague

The FAVS masterlist shows the Rome reading is not spread thin across
years; it detonates in a few months of 2024, nearly all rated 5:

| Read | Title | Author | Rating |
|------|-------|--------|--------|
| Jan 2024 | Death Throes of the Republic (Hardcore History #34–39) | Dan Carlin | 5 |
| Apr 07 2024 | Rubicon: The Last Years of the Roman Republic | Tom Holland | 5 |
| Apr 29 2024 | The Assassination of Julius Caesar | Michael Parenti | 5 |
| Jul 31 2024 | Caesar: Life of a Colossus | Adrian Goldsworthy | 5 |
| Jun 22 2024 | Augustus: First Emperor of Rome | Adrian Goldsworthy | 4 |
| Jul 30 2024 | Philip and Alexander | Adrian Goldsworthy | 4 |
| Jan 2024 | Complete Works of Plutarch | Plutarch | 5 |
| Jan 2024 | Lives of the Noble Romans | Plutarch | 5 |
| Jun 2024 | In the Name of Rome | Adrian Goldsworthy | 4 |

That is the standard modern Roman-Republic syllabus (Carlin → Holland →
Goldsworthy → Plutarch as primary source) read essentially back to back,
Goldsworthy alone accounting for four titles. The tags he attached —
`great-man-theory`, `caesar`, `roman-republic`, `top-shelf` — show he
read it as leadership-and-collapse material, not idle history.

## It became something he pushed on people

The distinctive thing (the pattern this wiki keeps under-recording) is
that the interest went *outward*. In spring 2024 he is actively
proselytizing it:

- **2024-03-27:** "You might like this. It's a series of videos
  explaining the story of Julius Caesar from the time he was elected to
  the Senate until his death" — sending someone a Caesar video series.
- **2024-04-09:** "Have you ever heard of the podcast Hardcore History
  with Dan Carlin" to one contact, and the same day "I just started
  listening to these dan Carlin podcasts" to another — evangelizing
  Carlin on two fronts at once.
- **2024-06-16:** "This new hardcore history on Alexander the Great is so
  good you're going to hear it 5000 times before I'm sick of it" — the
  binge-relistening habit stated outright, and the Alexander turn that
  lines up with reading Goldsworthy's *Philip and Alexander* that July.

## Why it matters

This is the analytical spine of the reading self applied to the deep
past. The same appetite that drives the Trump-era journalism shelf
([[wiki/interests/favorites/books/topics/politics]]) — power, betrayal,
the mechanics of how a republic degrades — is what pulls him to the fall
of Rome, and he explicitly connects the two eras (June 2024, sharing a
Romney/McConnell "end of the Senate" Atlantic piece in the same window).
It also rhymes with [[wiki/interests/gore-vidal]], another Rome-and-empire
fixation already documented. The "great man theory" tag across the
Goldsworthy books connects to the INTP "wounded genius" self-myth on
[[wiki/mind/profile/intp]]: Caesar, Alexander and Oppenheimer (also read
2023, rated 5, tagged `great-man-theory`) are all singular-figure-versus-
the-system stories.

**Gaps:** The pre-2024 ancient-history reading is lighter and its dates
are fuzzier ("Jan 2024" appears as a catch-all read-date on several
Plutarch/Carlin entries, likely a bulk backfill rather than literal). No
evidence in the sampled corpus of ancient history as a *childhood*
interest — it reads as an adult, mid-30s intensification, roughly
concurrent with the return to Pennsylvania.
