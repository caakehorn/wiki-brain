# The Dark Era — Opiates and the Body (2007–2008)

The two years between the November 2005 parental rupture and the September
2008 departure for Full Sail are when the chemical architecture that has
run continuously since actually began. Two body-level facts, previously
scattered across a stat line and a handful of message references, belong
together as one period: opiate onset, and a documented eating disorder.

## The opiate onset

At age 18–19, in the direct aftermath of his father's rehab admission and
his parents' divorce, Dan moved from the cocaine use already normalized in
the household ([[wiki/people/rick-frank]], [[wiki/people/suzanne-frank]])
to opiates — Roxicet, then oxycodone. Five months separated first use from
addiction. The self-profile frames the shift precisely: opiates served "for
internal regulation," and the operative question at the time was "how to
function without getting sick" — not euphoria-seeking but a stability
problem being solved with the tools available.

One specific choice is worth recording exactly because it complicates the
period's name: **heroin was explicitly rejected**, on aesthetic rather than
moral or risk grounds — it "felt dirty." The preference was for cleaner,
more clinical highs, and it held. This is a "heroin and opiates" era in the
sense that heroin was on the table and turned down, not one where it was
used. The stabilization did not arrive until Suboxone day-zero, February
17, 2010, at the tail end of the Full Sail window
([[wiki/timeline/periods/full-sail-2008-2010]],
[[wiki/health/chemical-architecture]]).

## The eating disorder

A second, previously undocumented body-history fact from the same broader
window: Dan had an eating disorder, referenced obliquely but repeatedly in
the [[wiki/people/annie-ulmer|Annie]] message corpus rather than in any dossier. Annie's own messages
confirm both a shared history and lasting physical effects — "Two people
with previous eating disorders together is something else" (Jan 9 2016)
and, describing herself, "I know that I fucked my stomach up from having
an eating disorder" (May 31 2016). A later message (Jul 15 2024, from a
different contact) has someone recognizing the same pattern in Dan directly:
"She's like you had an eating disorder didn't you? Bc I did too and I can
see myself in you." The self-profile separately frames adolescent cocaine
use (age 17–18, so slightly earlier, 2005–2006) as partly a weight-control
tool tied to bulimia — establishing that the eating disorder and the early
drug use were not separate threads but were serving overlapping functions
from the start.

## Reading the period together

Two coping mechanisms — a chemical one and a body-control one — both
trace to the same hinge point: the collapse of the family's stable
narrative in November 2005. Neither is framed anywhere in the source
material as resolved through treatment; the opiate arc resolves into
permanent chemical maintenance (Suboxone, 2010–present, zero relapses),
and the eating disorder's resolution, if any, is undocumented — it
surfaces only as something Annie and at least one other contact
recognized in him years later, never as its own worked-through history.

## Gaps

No primary source gives an exact date for the eating disorder's onset,
duration, or any point of resolution — the reconstruction above is built
entirely from oblique references in later message threads, not from any
direct account. The five-month first-use-to-addiction window for opiates
is stated but not dated to specific months within 2007.
