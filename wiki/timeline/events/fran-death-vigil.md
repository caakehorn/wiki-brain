# The Fran Vigil (March–April 2018)

In spring 2018 Dan and [[wiki/people/annie-ulmer|Annie]] saw
[[wiki/people/fran-coldren|Fran]] — his great-grandmother, whom they had
been paid to care for around the clock so she could stay in her house —
through her fall, her two-week destruction in a nursing home, and her death
in the Uniontown Hospital pediatric wing, where they were the only two
people in the room at the end. It is the single most intense documented
event of Dan's pre-corpus life, the emotional peak of the Annie decade, and
the episode the record treats as proof of what his devotion looks like when
it is actually engaged. Three minutes after she died, it started to snow.

## Sequence

**The fall ("Down Goes Frazier").** Dan found Fran on the floor of 117
Belmont Circle at 8:00 AM, screaming in agony and unable to stand. He
didn't know what to do; he filmed parts of the crisis — the record
preserves her saying "I don't want to die." His mother
[[wiki/people/suzanne-frank|Suz]] did not arrive to help until 10:00 AM:
she had been playing video keno all night. The two-hour keno delay stands
in the record as the defining contrast between the family's functional
caregiver and its nominal one.

**Institutional capture.** After brief stabilization Fran was mandated to
a nursing home — the exact outcome her standing directive ("stay in this
house as long as humanly possible") had been built to prevent, and which
she had predicted would finish her: within about two weeks her cognitive
and physical state totally failed. Footage from the period shows her in a
wheelchair working at the lap belt, still trying to get free.

**The pediatric death-shrine.** She was moved to a wing of the Uniontown
Hospital pediatric section to die — she and her husband Ira had been major
donors. Judge Fred Adams, Ira's old law partner and trustee of the estate,
mandated that someone be with her 24 hours a day; Dan and Annie held that
line while, per the record, the rest of the family stayed away. In the
final five days the hospital cut off her water as comfort care; she fought
through roughly four days of dehydration. On day four Dan stood in the
room blowing enormous clouds from a 110-watt SMOK box mod — his refusal to
sanitize the room's reality — while Suz puffed a "dainty" 10W vape in the
bathroom. A video from around this period, shown afterward to a friend
([[wiki/people/new-jim-shaffer]]), captures Fran smoking marijuana with
Dan directly and Annie laughing in the background — the friend's reaction
("that's solid, probably did her some good") treats it as of a piece with
the bong-as-"lovely vase" photo documented on [[wiki/people/fran-coldren]].

**The double loop.** On the morning of her final day Dan was caddying two
loops at Nemacolin in the freezing cold to keep his own cash flow alive —
manual labor for the golf world Fran had reigned over, hours before
sitting her deathbed.

**The eviction notice.** On March 29, 2018 — mid-vigil — Dan was served an
eviction notice by Dian V. Moore terminating his residency at
[[wiki/places/155-virginia-ave|155 Virginia Ave]] and demanding the keys:
the family/estate moving to liquidate the property before Fran was even
gone. The record treats it as the sharpest documented instance of the
"vertical" family acting against the person actually doing the caregiving.

**The end.** Fran died with only Dan and Annie present. Three minutes
later, heavy snow began falling outside Uniontown Hospital — an April
snowstorm the record keeps as the closing image.

## Why it matters

The vigil is load-bearing in three directions. For Dan's self-model, it is
the counter-evidence to the low-altruism reading: months of unglamorous,
round-the-clock care, held to the end. For the Annie record, it is the
high-water mark — the one extended episode where the two of them function
as a unit under real weight, which is part of why the bond survived so much
after. And for the family record, it crystallizes the pattern: the
functional sentinel doing the work while the estate's institutional actors
(the trustee, the eviction letter, the absent relatives) managed the
assets.
