# AI Video Essays (planned project)

A 2026 planning-stage project: Dan wants to make long-form (30-60
minute) video essays about AI, aimed at people who — like he describes
himself — are "AI evangelist[s]" without deep technical/CS background,
people who "look for a 'download' button to use github" and are missing
out on a technology they could easily be using. No script or published
video is recorded anywhere in the corpus; this page documents the
planning process itself, which is substantive enough to be worth
tracking.

## The structural template: Some More News

Dan's starting point is a structural "autopsy" of a Cody Johnston/*Some
More News* long-form essay, reverse-engineered into a reusable
five-element framework he explicitly requests in "bootloader" form for
his own future writing sessions: a one-sentence, falsifiable **thesis**;
a **conceit** (a structural frame that performs the argument rather than
decorating it — SMN's time-machine device); a 6-10-item **evidence
chain** of primary artifacts sequenced for cumulative pressure; at least
three **tonal modulation** points; and at least one **demonstration**
moment where the abstract argument is made visible rather than told.
The distilled document also maps a five-phase runtime architecture (hook
0-5 min, thesis establishment 5-12, evidence build 12-40, complication
40-50, resolution 50-60) and a pre-write checklist Dan says he wants
loaded at the start of every future essay-building session.

## The landscape gap he identifies

Asked to scan the existing AI-content space before committing to a
lane, the exchange maps four occupied clusters — technical/research
(Karpathy, 3Blue1Brown), tool-demo/news (Matt Wolfe, Fireship),
productivity (Sabrina Ramonov), and credentialed long-form interview
(Lex Fridman) — and identifies what Dan frames as the actual gap:
nobody making philosophical AI content **from inside the non-technical
user's subjective experience**, using personal-data demonstrations
("running your text logs through" a model) as "argument made visceral"
rather than tutorial content. This positions the planned project as a
public-facing extension of the same exocortex/AI-as-mirror framing
already documented on
[[wiki/mind/synthesis/ai-collaborative-analysis|the AI-collaboration
page]].

## The first pitch, and its own correction

Dan's first concrete idea: millennials have pattern-matched AI to
crypto and are missing out because of a category error — a claim he
frames partly as personal defense ("for as much of an AI evangelist as
I am... I'm not slop brained"). Pushed to stress-test the hypothesis
against real data before building on it, the research comes back
**against his premise**: adoption data shows millennials are not absent
from AI at all — they are arguably the power-user cohort, more likely
than Gen Z to report daily use and to say AI helps them "work smarter."
What the data does support is a sharper, more specific claim: millennials
use AI instrumentally while remaining deeply ambivalent about it,
resenting a technology they never got to encounter on their own terms
rather than through a VC hype narrative. Dan accepts the correction
rather than defending the original premise — a real-time instance of
requesting a check on his own idea and taking the result at face value,
including when it directly contradicted what he'd proposed.
