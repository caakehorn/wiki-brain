# Emaly Minerd

Emaly Minerd was a five-month, high-intensity relationship (`+17245525006`, 613 messages, September 2018 – January 2019) that started as a drug-sourcing contact, grew into something Emaly explicitly tried to make a real friendship, and collapsed exactly the way she warned it would — into the transactional, sex-first dynamic she'd spent months trying to avoid. It's one of the most emotionally dense threads in the corpus outside the primary relationships, and it ends badly, with Emaly delivering a genuinely prescient warning about where Dan's trajectory was heading.

## Who she was

Emaly managed a cell phone store, had a chronic illness requiring periodic transfusions (framed elsewhere as narcolepsy-adjacent), and was three years into a relationship with a boyfriend, Derek, that she describes as loving but strained — both of them, by her account, wanting things from the relationship the other couldn't provide. She had written a book she was proud of ("I poured my heart and soul into that book") and had attempted to get it published. The two apparently attended the same high school and may have crossed paths years earlier ("wish you would have said that to me in '05 or '06").

## The arc

The relationship opens purely transactional — Emaly buying Adderall, Xanax, kratom, and occasionally cocaine from Dan, negotiating prices, complaining about a dealer named Kevin Santore who wasted her time repeatedly. Within weeks the tone shifts: long, unguarded late-night conversations, personal disclosures (her illness, her relationship history, a near-breakup with Derek), and mutual flattery — Emaly telling Dan he reads as "a nice guy, and dare I even say sweet" despite his own self-description as "a 1% dirtbag."

By late November 2018 the relationship becomes in-person: Emaly meets both Dan and [[wiki/people/annie-ulmer|Annie]] together, likes Annie ("she's definitely one of the rare ones... I've never been able to have many friends that are girls"), and the three hang out repeatedly. She's aware of and explicitly discusses the Dan/Annie non-monogamous arrangement, asking pointed questions about how it works and whether it requires Annie to keep sleeping with other men for Dan to feel the way he does about her — one of the few outside voices in the corpus to name the arrangement's mechanics back to Dan directly. It's in this same window (November 28, 2018) that Dan gives her the fullest retrospective account anywhere in the corpus of how his then-current arrangement with [[wiki/people/alexis-armel|Alexis]] began — including the detail that he'd "initially" offered Alexis $3,000 to be "a plaything for Annie" — while also telling Emaly the two women's actual connection came together on its own; full account at [[wiki/timeline/events/annie-alexis-reunion-november-2018]].

## The refusal and the collapse

On December 6, 2018, Emaly draws an explicit line: she won't be "the kinky pawn" the couple needs to fulfill their relationship, tells Dan the only way she seems able to stay connected to him is through sex, and says she won't do that — she's put too many years into her relationship with Derek to risk it for someone who "just wants sex." She offers friendship on those terms and asks whether that's possible.

The answer, over the following weeks, is functionally no. A month-long silence (December 6 to January 7) breaks with Emaly reaching out again, saying she'd kept her distance out of respect for Dan and Annie but missed the friendship; Dan's reply apparently makes clear there's no room in his life for "friends who aren't hookups," which Emaly calls out directly and sadly: "Everyone needs emotional connection... you don't want to be friends with me because of how I look." She writes that she'd almost left Derek at one point and would have told Dan if it had happened — a disclosure that goes nowhere.

The final exchange, January 27, 2019, is the relationship's ugliest and most honest moment. Emaly offers Dan a copy of her book; his response — details not preserved on this side of the export, but reconstructable from her reaction — is an insult sharp enough that she spends a dozen messages defending the book, accusing him of cruelty, and telling him plainly: "you'll probably be dead in the next 15 years... living the dream." She names another burnout case, "Matt Caton," as a comparable cautionary example. She declares she's done, repeatedly. Then, in the same conversation, already having taken Xanax and what she calls "shitty coke" that night, she asks: "what's one harmless fuck" — collapsing, in real time, into precisely the dynamic she'd spent five months refusing.
