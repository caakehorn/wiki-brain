# Sam

Sam was Dan's New York cannabis delivery dealer for roughly the first
sixteen months at [[wiki/places/307-e-76th-st|307 E 76th St]] — a Lower
East Side operator (+13478611908, 374 surviving messages, May 2019 –
September 2020) running a small professional delivery service with
rotating strain menus, pickup-versus-delivery pricing, a delivery driver
with her own contact number, and Facebook-based customer verification.
The relationship was purely commercial, and its defining event is
commercial too: in February 2020, after Dan left him standing outside the
building twice in one day without answering, **Sam fired him as a
customer** — "Ok I'm leaving don't never hit this number" — and then
quietly un-fired him three months later when COVID thinned the customer
base ("You still in ny").

The thread survives one-sided — every row is marked Received and, unlike
most of the corpus, that appears literally true here: Dan's side shows up
only where Sam reacts to it (Liked/Laughed quotations), which is also
where the page's few Dan-voiced details come from.

## The operation

First contact ran on referral protocol: "Send me your Facebook link for
verification," then "Accept trippy Santa" — Sam's screening identity —
before the menu came through. The menus themselves are the thread's
skeleton, arriving every few weeks: flower by strain (Gorilla Glue,
Skittles, Sunset Sherbet, Maui Wowie, La Confidential, Wedding Cake, Lava
Cake, Slurricane), full-gram Kingpen and Smartcart oil cartridges, and
300–500mg edibles. Pricing was posted like a shop: an eighth $50–60, a
quarter $100–120 ("1/4 100$" is the thread's most repeated line), carts
$60–80, always cheaper on pickup than delivery. Logistics were
professional for the scale: a Toyota Camry double-parked out front, a
"delivery lady" texting from 3479515354, referral discounts ("Bring
Refferal's I'll hook u up"), and meeting-point fallbacks at Sam's
part-time dry-cleaner job at 61st and Lexington.

The first buy (May 24, 2019) set the pattern: Dan rode to Sam's own block
— "296 side, 2nd floor staircase," off Delancey — on his Boosted board
("i just need to let my boosted board charge up for like 10 more mins,"
preserved in Sam's Like), paid $130 cash, and pitched himself as a
regular: "awesome dude i'll need that size order once or twice a week."
Thereafter Sam delivered uptown, and the thread is a metronome of
"eta 30 min / come down / in front of 307."

## The arc: regular, fired, rehired, forgotten

Through late 2019 the cadence is steady — roughly monthly-to-biweekly
orders, occasional misses handled politely on both sides. The rupture
comes on February 23, 2020, and it is the one place the thread turns
emotional: Sam drives to 76th Street mid-morning on a confirmed order,
stands outside sending fourteen escalating messages ("I'm in front your
building / Should I leave? / Damn u can't even text me back"), leaves,
agrees to return that night, gets silence again, and quits: "And never
waste my time again… don't never hit this number."

What makes the incident structurally interesting is that it is the same
customer-side behavior the wider corpus documents from the other
direction. The [[wiki/mind/synthesis/supply-network|supply network]]'s
reliability inversion says distance disciplines suppliers — the
professional [[wiki/people/menore|Menore]] answered 99.3% of requests
while friend-suppliers failed freely. Sam's walk-off shows the same
discipline running the other way: a professional also *enforces* on the
buyer, where Dan's friend-suppliers and family absorbed his
unresponsiveness without consequence. Menore and Sam served the same
apartment in the same years with opposite tolerance for the same
customer, which is why the pair make a clean bracket around what a
transactional bond does and doesn't put up with.

The firing didn't hold — but the re-approach came from Sam, on May 14,
2020, deep in the [[wiki/timeline/periods/covid-era-2020|COVID
spring]]: "You still in ny," followed by a fresh price list. The pandemic
stretch that follows is the thread at its most texture-rich: deliveries
negotiated around ride availability and a car that "started coming out
smoke," Dan's flaky cell service covered by a standing instruction
preserved in Sam's Like — "just ring #17 and i will fly down" — and
small kindnesses ("U have 70$ I can give u extra gram if u like").

The last exchange (September 10, 2020) closes the loop with an
unsentimental symmetry: Sam runs the verification script on Dan as if he
were a stranger — "Send me your Facebook link for verification" — then
catches himself mid-message: "Don't send / I remember." One final
quarter of Orange Cream is negotiated, a meetup is pushed to tomorrow,
and the thread simply ends. Nothing marks it as a goodbye; the customer
relationship just stops being needed.
