# Anita (BFS Foods GM)

Anita was the general manager of the BFS Foods gas-station side — the
Bruceton Farm Service convenience store paired with a company-owned
Little Caesars in the same Uniontown building — during Dan's roughly
month-long cashier stint in spring 2026, and she is the locus of agency
in the [[wiki/work/bfs-foods|drawer-shortage dispute]] that ended the
job. She originated the $50 shortage claim, and she holds every lever the
dispute turns on: the morning deposit reconciliation, the schedule (Dan's
hours went 36 → 7 the same day he questioned the demand), the discipline
paperwork, and the store's narrative to corporate. Brandon Hill delivered
the personal demand, but the claim was hers.

## The confabulation pattern

What the record documents about Anita is a consistent tell: her
explanations are *narrative-shaped, not evidence-shaped*. Asked what
caused the $50 shortage, she told Dan "it was the gas, foodstamps, and
lottery" — a triplet of plausible-sounding categories, one of which
(foodstamps/EBT) is mechanically incapable of shorting a cash drawer at
all, since it settles electronically with no cash moving through the
till. Lottery and EBT are, moreover, the store's *specifically cataloged*
categories, each with its own receipt trail; a real diagnosis touching
them would have produced a receipt number, a transaction ID, a timestamp.
She produced category names and no pointers, then reverted to "I don't
know what happened yet." That is the fingerprint of someone who had a
number and back-filled causes under it, not someone who traced a
variance. Dan's own read: "the composition of her three categories is the
fingerprint of a confabulation, not a diagnosis."

## The Timmy precedent

Anita's pattern is established independently of the drawer dispute by the
[[wiki/people/timmy|Timmy]] episode two weeks earlier. Timmy, an underage
Little Caesars employee, was banned from the store for playing its slot
machines on the clock and blocking the regular gamblers. When the Little
Caesars manager [[wiki/people/kim|Kim]] pulled camera footage to document
the gambling, it incidentally caught **Anita selling Timmy a vape while he
was underage** — a licensing liability that nearly got her fired. She
survived, then reportedly kept selling him vapes. The revealing part is
the blame-pivot: Anita told Timmy that *Kim* had reported him, and when
Timmy confronted Kim and she denied it, Anita switched the story to
*"Daniel"* — a coworker with zero involvement. Maddox and Dakota
corroborated the pivot; Maddox's unprompted verdict was "she's always
making everything so crazy." The Timmy arc is the template the drawer
dispute then re-runs: build a narrative backward, run it past someone, see
if it sticks.

## Why she matters to the architecture

Anita is a clean small-scale exhibit for
[[wiki/mind/synthesis/vertical-authority-skepticism]]: an authority whose
power over Dan came entirely from controlling the levers (deposit,
schedule, discipline) rather than from records, whose claims did not
survive procedural scrutiny, and who is nonetheless nearly untouchable at
the bottom of a corporate structure that only hears her version. The
strategy analysis was explicit that her genuine exposure — the on-camera
vape sale to a minor — was *not* to be weaponized: deploying it would
convert Dan's clean posture (refusing an illegal demand) into extortion
risk and invite symmetric review of his own daily in-store customer
handoffs. The competent move against her was procedural and defensive
(preserve documents, refuse to pay, pursue partial unemployment), not a
takedown.
