# Alex Frank

Dan's cousin — described by Dan as "semi-famous" — and, per public
sources, a real working journalist and editor based in Brooklyn covering
music, fashion, and culture. He was Deputy Editor at The FADER and Deputy
Culture Editor at Vogue.com, and has bylined at Vogue, The FADER,
Pitchfork, ELLE, Dazed & Confused, New York Magazine, Vulture, Fantastic
Man, the Village Voice, GQ, V Man, and The New York Times Style Magazine,
with celebrity profiles and interviews spanning Aretha Franklin, Bjork,
Tyler the Creator, Andre 3000, SZA, Young Thug, Kacey Musgraves, Virgil
Abloh, RuPaul, and many others.

The connection to Dan runs through the paternal Frank line: Dan's
grandfather and Alex's grandfather were **brothers**. That makes Alex Dan's
cousin (first cousin once removed) and puts Morley Jay Frank — Alex's
"Uncle Morley" — as the shared great-uncle on the paternal side. The
Frank-family funeral in Alex's essay is family material to Dan, not
distant gossip.

## Alex's first-person account

Alex's own first-person essay account of the Morley funeral cookie story —
quoted verbatim:

> I always tell this story about my Uncle Morley. He was a real pisser, you know? You might have an Uncle Morley in your family, but this guy made the mold. His humor was a mix of self-deprecation, sexual harassment, and insult, but his endearing, crooked half-smile (my Grandpa had the exact same grin) kept the smart, well-humored ones from being too hurt by his jokes. I was too young to quite get it, but I did get a sense of no-bullshit from him that children always respond to. Anyway, there's this diner in Pittsburgh, Eat'n Park that's famous for making frosted smily face cookies. They've been doing it for decades, and they're one of those Pittsburgh symbols that unite us (The Original, Primanti's sandwiches, Mineo's: they're all food, of course, we're fat, okay?). Anyway, everyday Uncle Morley stopped at the Eat'n Park by his house for a coffee, stared at the shelves and shelves of Eat'n Park cookies mocking him with their smiles and asked the cashier one thing: "I'm having a shit day, can't you be fair and make me a frownie face cookie?" They laughed off his self-deprecating routine like anyone would. But he asked everyday. And they never gave it to him.
>
> When Uncle Morley died, his wife Sis had boxes of frownie face cookies made up from the local Eat'n Park for his funeral party. When we all walked in and saw the inverted symbol strewn amongst the more generic catered items, it expressed a certain kind of familial understanding that comes from generations of genetic personality traits, like a fucked up sense of humor and melancholy lense on life. The more earnest funeral goers didn't get the joke, I suspect. But the smart, well-humored ones did. And they're the only ones you want at the party anyway.
