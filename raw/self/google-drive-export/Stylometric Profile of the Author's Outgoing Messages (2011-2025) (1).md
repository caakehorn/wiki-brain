## Lexical Usage Patterns

**Core Vocabulary and Diction:** The author’s texts are dominated by first- and second-person pronouns (“I”, “you”, “u”), conveying a personal, conversational tone. Similarly, **“like”** is often inserted in a colloquial manner (e.g. **“Ah I feel so bad and like I sounded weird”**[\[3\]](about:blank)), reflecting a spontaneous, speech-like quality in the messaging. ## Morphosyntactic Characteristics

**Sentence Structure and Length:** The author’s messages tend toward **short, simple sentence structures**, often representing a single clause or thought per text bubble. Many messages are sentence fragments or a few words, reflecting how one might speak in quick bursts. For example, the author often breaks a multi-clause thought into successive messages: **“She's still...here....ughhhh”** (first message) then **“Can I like...tell her?”** (follow-up message seconds later)[\[35\]](about:blank). Each text on its own is a fragment, but together they form a complete idea. This fragmentary delivery gives the conversation a **stream-of-consciousness feel**, as if we are seeing each thought as it occurs. When sentences are longer, they are usually strung together with simple conjunctions (“and”, “but”) or ellipses rather than formal complex syntax. For instance, **“But I just like...ah...this feels so important and if I need to explain like why I was sad before and happy now...”**[\[36\]](about:blank) uses multiple conjunctions and pauses (“...”) instead of breaking into separate sentences or using semicolons. The result is a run-on quality that mirrors spoken language. There is little evidence of structured, multi-clause sentences with subordination; instead, the author often sends a **rapid series of short clauses** in separate texts rather than embedding them in one long sentence.

**Contractions and Colloquial Forms:** The author nearly always uses **contracted forms** (I'm, you're, can't, it's) instead of full formal words, in line with the informal style. For example, **“I’m not gonna lie”**, **“you’re perfect”**, **“it’s gonna be fine”** are typical (many instances occur, e.g. **“Okay I'm walking into the house now. The author also writes **“’em”** for *them* occasionally and **“ya”** for *you* (see **“If ya know what's good for ya”**[\[40\]](about:blank)). These non-standard contractions and phonetic spellings (like *“tryna”* for trying to, *“kinda”* for kind of, *“gonna”*, *“wanna”*, etc.) are prevalent, giving the text an *authentically spoken* flavor. Standard grammar rules are loosened in favor of phonetic ease – for example, the author says **“I told you that i totally understand”**[\[38\]](about:blank), leaving “I” in lowercase “i” mid-sentence, likely due to fast typing or disabled auto-correct. There’s also inconsistent agreement and dropped words in some sentences, suggesting an *impromptu quality* where the author is typing as thoughts come, sometimes neglecting minor grammatical consistency. Despite these quirks, the overall syntax remains clear – the author relies on *simple clauses, conversational connectors, and common contractions* to communicate fluidly.

**Use of Questions and Commands:** The author frequently poses questions, often in a rapid or rhetorical manner, and these are usually **informally structured**. It’s common to see multiple question marks or combined punctuation for emphasis in questions (e.g. **“how can you tell?\!?\!”**[\[41\]](about:blank) showing excitement and inquiry together). Commands and suggestions are phrased casually, often starting with “let’s” or expressed as friendly imperatives: **“Meet me at marathon”**[\[44\]](about:blank) or **“Just let me know and I will always be there”**[\[12\]](about:blank). The tone is invitational rather than authoritative, aligning with the cooperative, affectionate context of most messages.

**Grammar and Negation:** The texts show a preference for **informal negation**. The author almost never uses “do not” or “cannot,” opting instead for **“don’t,” “can’t,” “won’t,” “ain’t”** etc. In fact, double negatives or colloquial negation slip in occasionally for effect: *“that shit ain’t no jokes”*[\[45\]](about:blank). Grammar “mistakes” that are typical in speech are present, though they seem intentional or at least not corrected – for example, **“If I faint just revive me and keep going”**[\[46\]](about:blank) (missing a comma before “just” as it’s a spoken run-on), or **“My heart hurts from not seeing you… How long do u think you’ll be out for?”**[\[47\]](about:blank) ending a clause with a preposition (“out for”). These lend authenticity to the conversational style. The author does not appear concerned with formal grammar rules like not ending sentences with prepositions or using whom vs. who, etc. The messaging grammar is **driven by clarity and emotion, not formality**. When needed, the author can produce grammatically complex sentences – for instance, when writing a serious missive, they managed a fully structured sentence: **“I’ve been trying really hard not to send you any emotionally loaded texts while you’re working.”**[\[48\]](about:blank). But such sentences stand out as deliberate and likely composed with extra care, whereas the bulk of the corpus favors *simple, loosely connected syntax*. In summary, the morphosyntactic profile is one of **simplicity and speech-mimicking structure**: short clauses, heavy use of contractions, colloquial shorthand, and grammar that bends toward expressive ease rather than textbook rules.
