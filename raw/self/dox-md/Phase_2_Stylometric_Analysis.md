# Phase 2 Stylometric Analysis: Contextual Adaptation of Writing Style

## Romantic vs. Platonic Interaction Styles

**Vocabulary & Terms of Address:** The author's language shifts noticeably between romantic and platonic conversations. In romantic texts with his partner (Annie), he employs endearments and affectionate slang like "sweetie," "bb/bby" (baby), and playful pet names. For instance, he replies to a loving message with "i love you too sweetie," using a term of endearment he never uses with friends. In contrast, with friends he opts for casual buddy language—often calling them "dude" or using colloquial interjections. In one friendly exchange he writes, "my bad dude i forgot all about u," apologizing informally. He also greets and summons friends with "yo" or "hey dude" in ways he wouldn't with a romantic partner. This shows a clear adaptation: romantic messages include intimate nicknames and pronounced warmth, whereas platonic messages favor relaxed slang and peer-to-peer address.

**Affection vs. Banter:** In romantic interactions, the author's tone is markedly affectionate and emotionally expressive. He frequently expresses love and longing. By contrast, platonic chats (with friends or family) lean on humor, updates, and light insults as bonding. With romantic partners he never uses terms like "dawg" or "bro"; instead prefers "love bug" or the partner's name with endearment.

**Emotion Words and Topics:** Romantic texts revolve around personal feelings, future plans together, and mutual care. In platonic contexts, especially with male friends, deep emotional vocabulary is rarer; conversations skew to external topics (music, drugs, plans) or shared humor.

**Pronoun & Form Changes:** He shifts the shorthand for "you" depending on context and over time — "u" appears more in intimate excited early messages and casual friend exchanges; by 2023 nearly all "you" in serious romantic contexts. Capital "I" is more likely preserved in serious/apologetic messages to his girlfriend.

**Conversation Cadence:** Romantic chats show rapid, continuous cadence throughout the day with bursty back-and-forth. Platonic exchanges are more sporadic and context/purpose-driven, though can burst during collaborative excitement (e.g. investigating a camgirl together, 2020-03-23).

## Secure vs. Volatile Emotional Moments

**Calm vs. Agitated Tone:** Secure moments: relaxed, cooperative, caring undertone ("I love you, have fun. Try to relax"). Volatile moments: abrupt, stressed punctuation/brevity ("Please stop! I don't need you to remind me to worry about shit…not in front of her").

**Punctuation as Emotional Gauge:** Stacked punctuation (???, !!!) is a reliable tell for heightened emotional state. Multiple question marks signal urgent need for explanation during conflict; absent in secure exchanges.

**Sentence Structure & Length:** Secure/happy exchanges: longer composed sentences. Volatile: rapid-fire fragmented messages, omitted subjects/verbs, one thought per message ("staccato style").

**Vocabulary and Cursing:** Secure mode: profanity as intensifier/jest, not directed at someone. Volatile: profanity turns pointed/negative-intensifier aimed at the other person; all-caps for shouted emphasis words.

**Emotional Reassurance Patterns:** After volatile spikes, shifts into remorseful/reassuring mode — repeated "I'm sorry," "I love you," "I promise," formal spelling/capitalization to underscore sincerity. This apology-flood pattern doesn't occur in platonic volatile moments (rare/brief arguments with friends).

## Playful vs. Serious Conversations

**Humor Markers:** Elongated letters ("soooo," "Noooooo"), expressive spelling ("yaaaas," "OMG"), emoji-heavy, role-play/hyperbole, mini scenarios and exaggeration.

**Clarity in Serious Discussions:** Drops emojis/quirky spelling for plain procedural sentences, even with romantic partner when discussing finances/health seriously.

**Switching Codes:** Same conversation, same night, can show him joking with a friend then immediately switching to sincere/compassionate register with his partner minutes later.

**Slang and References:** Playful mode: pop-culture/niche references (Mitch Hedberg, Jimi Hendrix), slangy spelling ("fookin'"). Serious mode: literal, universal language, no slang toward authority-type recipients (mother, boss-figures).

## Supportive vs. Conflict-Based Exchanges

**Supportive Reassurance Style:** Second-person focus, positive imperatives, future-oriented optimism/inclusivity ("we're a team," "we'll get through this"), building the other person up.

**Linguistic Soothing vs. Escalation:** Conflict language becomes confrontational — accusatory second-person statements, defensive first-person justification, omitted politeness qualifiers, bare imperatives ("Leave now").

**Length & Frequency:** Supportive messages: longer, more detailed, coherent. Conflict messages: machine-gun rapid short fragments, overlapping simultaneous sends.

**Content Focus:** Supportive: solutions/understanding, proactive attentiveness ("Anything I can do?"). Conflict: grievances, rhetorical/pointed questions, self-justification/blame.

**Emotion Indicators:** Supportive: empathy markers, repeated "I love you!!!" Conflict: curt ellipsis responses ("...okay"), sardonic "lol" only, controlled irritation via misspelling without softening emoji.

## Summary

The author's linguistic fingerprint dynamically flexes across interpersonal contexts — informality and intensifiers are core traits, but tenderness/curtness, slang filtering, punctuation and structure all modulate by relationship type and emotional tenor. He code-switches between devoted boyfriend, chill friend, concerned son, and combative partner registers, each with distinctive stylistic signatures — not a static habit set but a context-dependent palette.
