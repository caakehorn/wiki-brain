**The 2015 Uniontown trough (3,386).** Same problem, sharper, because we *know* an account migration occurred eleven months earlier (May 19–22, 2014, timestamped to the minute). **The city-effect claim.** This is the big one. The browser analysis's most confident biographical inference is that "attention runs hotter, broader, and faster in New York" — built on the 2018–2019 climb to the all-time peak (9,927) coinciding with the NYC-2 move, against the Uniontown trough. The metabolic rate doesn't change when the city changes. If consumption intensity is city-invariant on YouTube, the prior on it being city-variant in search drops considerably, and the annual search volumes start looking like *days-active* and *routing* effects rather than intensity effects.

The test is cheap and the browser corpus already contains the raw material: recompute search actions per active day, per year, the same way the YouTube pass did. The corpus-wide figure is 20.2 actions per active day across 5,391 active days. If the per-active-day figure is roughly flat across the NYC peaks and the Uniontown troughs — and the YouTube precedent says to expect exactly that — then the city effect dissolves into a coverage effect, and one of the more flattering inferences in the stack ("New York activates you") gets replaced by something more interesting and more characteristic: **the appetite is constant everywhere; only the routing and the recorded surface change.** That would make the consumption metabolism the single most era-, city-, relationship-, and platform-invariant fact about you in twenty years of data.
