## PART II: CONSTITUTIONAL ANALYSIS OF THE TRAFFIC STOP

The threshold issue in any DUI case is whether the initial traffic stop violated the Fourth Amendment (U.S. Constitution) or Article I, Section 8 (Pennsylvania Constitution). If the stop was unconstitutional, all subsequent evidence—including observations, statements, field sobriety tests, and the refusal—must be suppressed as "fruit of the poisonous tree."

### A. The Investigable vs. Non-Investigable Framework

#### 1. The Salter/Feczko Distinction

Pennsylvania courts distinguish between two categories of Vehicle Code violations:

**Investigable Offenses (Reasonable Suspicion Standard):**
- Violations where the stop serves an investigatory purpose
- The officer needs to interact with the driver to confirm or dispel suspicion
- Example: Suspected DUI based on erratic driving (stop allows observation of eyes, breath, speech)
- Standard: Reasonable suspicion that a violation is occurring

**Non-Investigable Offenses (Probable Cause Standard):**
- Violations that are complete the moment observed
- The stop yields no additional evidence regarding the violation itself
- Examples: Running a red light, speeding (captured on radar), equipment violations
- Standard: Probable cause that a violation occurred

**Controlling Authority:**
- *Commonwealth v. Salter*, 121 A.3d 987 (Pa. Super. 2015)
- *Commonwealth v. Feczko*, 10 A.3d 1285 (Pa. Super. 2010)

#### 2. Application to Ulmer's Stop

The stated bases for stopping Ms. Ulmer are:
1. **High beams (§ 4306)** - Non-investigable: Once observed, the violation is complete
2. **Lane straddling (§ 3111/3309)** - Non-investigable: The officer sees the deviation; stopping adds nothing

**Therefore:** The Commonwealth must prove the officer possessed **probable cause**—not merely reasonable suspicion—to initiate the stop. This is a significantly higher burden.

### B. High Beam Analysis (75 Pa.C.S. § 4306)

#### 1. Statutory Framework

**§ 4306(a) Requirement:**
> "Whenever the driver of a vehicle approaches an oncoming vehicle within 500 feet, the driver shall use the low beam of light."

**§ 4306(c)(2) Exception:**
> "Nothing in this section shall limit drivers from flashing high beams at oncoming vehicles as a warning of roadway emergencies or other dangerous or hazardous conditions ahead."

#### 2. The "Momentary Flash" Doctrine: *Commonwealth v. Kostishak*

**Case:** *Commonwealth v. Kostishak*, 2017 WL 10604620 (Lycoming C.P. 2017)

**Facts:**
- Defendant flashed high beams for approximately one second
- Distance of 100 feet from officer
- Officer initiated traffic stop based solely on this flash

**Holding:** Evidence suppressed. Momentary activation does not provide probable cause.

**Reasoning:**
1. The statutory exception explicitly permits flashing for warnings
2. A prudent officer must consider whether a brief flash is an attempt to communicate a lawful warning
3. Drivers commonly flash beams to signal that oncoming vehicles have their high beams on
4. Because the statute permits this conduct, a momentary flash is **ambiguous**
5. Ambiguous conduct cannot establish probable cause

**Application to Ulmer:**
- **Critical Task:** Review dashcam footage to determine duration of high beam usage
- **If momentary/flash:** Strong suppression argument under *Kostishak*
- **If sustained:** Proceed to *Bush* analysis

#### 3. The "Sustained Usage" Standard: *Commonwealth v. Bush*

**Case:** *Commonwealth v. Bush*, 166 A.3d 1278 (Pa. Super. 2017)

**Facts:**
- Defendant activated high beams while approaching police cruiser
- Maintained high beams as he passed the vehicle
- Appellant argued officer must prove he was "blinded"

**Holding:** Stop upheld. Sustained use violates § 4306 regardless of actual effect.

**Reasoning:**
1. The statutory language is strict and unambiguous
2. Requires low beams within 500 feet, period
3. Officer need not prove subjective impairment or being "blinded"
4. Sustained usage distinguishable from momentary flash

**Application to Ulmer:**
- **If high beams remained engaged:** *Bush* is harmful precedent
- **Defense must pivot:** Argue officer's observation was mistaken, automatic high beams activated unintentionally, or focus on lane violation weakness

#### 4. Strategic Analysis: High Beams

| Scenario | Applicable Case | Probable Cause? | Defense Strategy |
|----------|----------------|-----------------|------------------|
| Flash (1-2 seconds) | *Kostishak* | **NO** | Motion to suppress; statutory exception for warnings |
| Brief activation (3-5 seconds) | *Kostishak* (arguably) | **Unclear** | Argue ambiguous conduct insufficient for PC |
| Sustained (6+ seconds) | *Bush* | **YES** | Challenge officer's observation; focus on lane violations |

### C. Lane Deviation Analysis (75 Pa.C.S. § 3111 vs. § 3309)

#### 1. The Charging Decision: Why § 3111?

The officer charged **§ 3111(a) - Obedience to Traffic-Control Devices** rather than **§ 3309(1) - Driving in Single Lane**. This is strategically significant.

**§ 3309(1) Standard:**
> A vehicle shall be driven "as nearly as practicable entirely within a single lane."

**Key Language:** "As nearly as practicable" acknowledges that minor, momentary deviations are inevitable and do not constitute violations absent a safety hazard.

**§ 3111(a) Standard:**
> The driver shall obey the instructions of any applicable official traffic-control device.

**Why Officers Prefer § 3111:**
- Avoids the "practicability" language of § 3309
- No explicit requirement to show a safety hazard
- Attempts to circumvent *Gleason* protections

**Defense Argument:** Courts examine the **underlying conduct**, not just the statute cited. If the conduct was lane weaving/straddling, § 3309 jurisprudence applies regardless of how the officer charged it. Applying § 3111 to lane deviations renders the specific protections of § 3309 meaningless, violating statutory construction principles.

#### 2. The "Momentary and Minor" Standard: *Commonwealth v. Gleason*

**Case:** *Commonwealth v. Gleason*, 785 A.2d 983 (Pa. 2001)

**Facts:**
- Defendant crossed solid white fog line by 6-8 inches
- Duration: A second or two
- Occurred over a quarter-mile stretch on a deserted road
- Officer initiated stop based on this deviation

**Holding:** Stop suppressed. No probable cause for § 3309 violation.

**Reasoning:**
1. § 3309(1)'s "as nearly as practicable" language is intentional
2. Minor, momentary deviations are inevitable in normal driving
3. Such deviations do not constitute violations absent a showing of safety hazard
4. De minimis deviations on deserted roads create no hazard

**Key Quote:**
> "The statute recognizes that it is not always practicable to keep a vehicle entirely within a single lane... We find that [the defendant's] vehicle touching the fog line for a second or two over such a distance is not, in and of itself, a violation."

**Application to Ulmer:**
- **Critical Inquiry:** Which lane markers were involved?
- **If white fog line or same-direction lane divider:** Strong *Gleason* argument
- **If momentary straddling:** Emphasize brevity and lack of hazard
- **Strengthen argument if:** Late night, no traffic, distraction from ankle pain

#### 3. The "Center Line Exception": *Commonwealth v. Enick*

**Case:** *Commonwealth v. Enick*, 70 A.3d 843 (Pa. Super. 2013)

**Facts:**
- Driver crossed double yellow center line one time
- No oncoming traffic present
- Appellant argued no hazard existed without actual oncoming vehicles

**Holding:** Stop upheld. Crossing center line provides probable cause.

**Reasoning:**
1. *Gleason* distinguished: applies only to fog lines and same-direction lane markers
2. Crossing the center double yellow line is **inherently hazardous**
3. Places vehicle in path of opposing traffic by definition
4. Actual presence of oncoming vehicles is irrelevant
5. The hazard is presumed from the nature of the violation itself

**Application to Ulmer:**
- **Critical determination:** Identify which lines were involved
- **From affidavit:** "Straddling lane designators between straight and right turn lanes"
- **Analysis:** This describes same-direction lanes (likely white dashed or solid lines), NOT the center double yellow
- **Conclusion:** *Gleason* should apply, not *Enick*

#### 4. Strategic Framework: Lane Violations

**Defense Checklist:**

□ **Obtain dashcam footage** - Visual evidence of exact lane position  
□ **Identify specific lane markers** - White vs. yellow; solid vs. dashed; fog line vs. divider  
□ **Measure duration** - Momentary (1-2 seconds) vs. sustained  
□ **Document traffic conditions** - Deserted road strengthens *Gleason*  
□ **Establish alternative cause** - Distraction/pain from broken ankle  
□ **Challenge § 3111 charging** - Argue § 3309 protections apply to underlying conduct

**Probable Cause Matrix:**

| Lane Type | Duration | Traffic | *Gleason* Applies? | Probable Cause? |
|-----------|----------|---------|-------------------|-----------------|
| White fog line | Momentary (1-2 sec) | None/Light | ✓ YES | ❌ NO |
| White lane divider | Brief (3-5 sec) | None/Light | ✓ YES (likely) | ⚠️ Uncertain |
| White lines | Sustained (6+ sec) | Moderate | ⚠️ Unclear | ✓ Likely YES |
| Double yellow | Any duration | Any | ❌ NO (*Enick*) | ✓ YES |

### D. Comparative Case Law Analysis

| Case | Citation | Facts | Holding | Relevance to Ulmer |
|------|----------|-------|---------|-------------------|
| **Gleason** | 785 A.2d 983 (Pa. 2001) | 6-8 inch fog line crossing for 1-2 seconds, deserted road | **SUPPRESSED** - Momentary/minor deviation insufficient | **PRIMARY DEFENSE** if straddling was white lines, brief, and minor |
| **Enick** | 70 A.3d 843 (Pa. Super. 2013) | Single crossing of double yellow center line | **UPHELD** - Center line crossing inherently hazardous | **HARMFUL** if Ulmer crossed center line; distinguishable if only white lines |
| **Kostishak** | 2017 WL 10604620 (Lycoming C.P. 2017) | 1-second high beam flash at 100 feet | **SUPPRESSED** - Ambiguous; consistent with warning | **PRIMARY DEFENSE** if high beams were momentary |
| **Bush** | 166 A.3d 1278 (Pa. Super. 2017) | Sustained high beam use passing police | **UPHELD** - Strict liability; no "blinding" required | **HARMFUL** if high beams sustained; focus on lane arguments |
| **Salter** | 121 A.3d 987 (Pa. Super. 2015) | Stop for non-investigable summary offense | Requires **probable cause**, not reasonable suspicion | **FOUNDATIONAL** - Establishes higher burden for this stop |

### E. Suppression Motion Strategy

**Motion to Suppress Evidence: Traffic Stop Lacking Probable Cause**

**Legal Standard:** For non-investigable offenses (high beams, lane deviations), the Commonwealth must prove the officer possessed probable cause to believe a violation occurred.

**Arguments:**

**1. High Beams (§ 4306):**
- Momentary activation consistent with statutory exception for warnings (*Kostishak*)
- Ambiguous conduct cannot establish probable cause
- Officer must rule out lawful explanations before stopping vehicle
- Alternative explanation: Warning officer his high beams were on, or signaling hazard

**2. Lane Deviation (§ 3111/§ 3309):**
- Straddling same-direction lane dividers is momentary and minor (*Gleason*)
- No safety hazard created on lightly trafficked street at 12:49 AM
- "As nearly as practicable" language protects de minimis deviations
- Alternative explanation: Distraction/pain from broken ankle, adjusting position at intersection
- § 3111 misapplied: statute governs signals/signs, not lane markings

**3. Cumulative Analysis:**
- Even if each observation alone insufficient, their combination must establish PC
- Two ambiguous, de minimis observations do not cumulate to probable cause
- Officer's suspicion of DUI cannot bootstrap insufficient traffic violations into lawful stop

**Requested Relief:**
- Suppress all evidence obtained as a result of unlawful stop
- Dismiss all charges as fruit of the poisonous tree

## PART III: PROBABLE CAUSE FOR ARREST AND THE MEDICAL IMPAIRMENT FACTOR

Assuming the traffic stop survives suppression, the next constitutional threshold is the arrest itself. Did the officer possess probable cause to believe Ms. Ulmer was driving under the influence? In typical DUI cases, this determination relies heavily on Standardized Field Sobriety Tests (SFSTs). The broken ankle fundamentally alters this calculus.

### A. The Invalidation of Psychomotor Testing

#### 1. NHTSA Standards and Medical Contraindications

The National Highway Traffic Safety Administration (NHTSA) developed three standardized field sobriety tests:

1. **Horizontal Gaze Nystagmus (HGN)** - Eye tracking test
2. **Walk-and-Turn (WAT)** - Divided attention/balance test
3. **One-Leg Stand (OLS)** - Balance and instruction-following test

**NHTSA Manual - Medical Contraindications:**

The NHTSA DWI Detection and Standardized Field Sobriety Testing manual explicitly warns:

> "Individuals with certain medical conditions, physical impairments, or who are overweight may have difficulty performing the psychophysical tests. Such conditions include... leg injuries, ankle injuries... These individuals may be unable to complete the tests successfully regardless of sobriety."

**Medical Invalidity for Broken Ankle:**

- **Walk-and-Turn:** Requires walking heel-to-toe on a straight line, turning, and returning
  - Physically impossible with broken ankle
  - Pain, instability, and protective guarding will mimic intoxication "clues"
  - Swaying, using arms for balance, stepping off line are expected behaviors with ankle fracture

- **One-Leg Stand:** Requires standing on one leg for 30 seconds
  - Medically contraindicated with lower extremity fracture
  - Cannot bear weight on injured ankle
  - Hopping, swaying, putting foot down are injury-related, not intoxication indicators

**Conclusion:** The officer correctly did not administer WAT and OLS. However, their absence creates a significant evidentiary gap for the Commonwealth.

#### 2. The Resulting Evidentiary Vacuum

**Typical DUI Probable Cause Formula:**
- Driving pattern (weaving, speed, etc.)
- Physical observations (odor, eyes, speech)
- Field sobriety test performance (WAT, OLS, HGN)
- Statements/admissions
- PBT result (if obtained)

**Ulmer Probable Cause Formula:**
- Driving pattern: ⚠️ **Arguably de minimis** (high beams, minor lane straddling)
- Physical observations: ✓ Odor of alcohol, watery eyes, "dazed" appearance
- Field sobriety tests: ❌ WAT and OLS **excluded**; HGN only
- Statements: ✓ "I have not drank that much" / "Tito and Soda"
- PBT result: ❌ **Unable to complete**

**Critical Issue:** With two of three SFSTs excluded, probable cause rests heavily on HGN and subjective observations. This creates vulnerability.

### B. Alternative Explanations: The Broken Ankle Defense

Every observation the officer attributed to intoxication has an equally plausible innocent explanation: the broken ankle and the circumstances of the stop.

| Officer's Observation | Intoxication Explanation | Broken Ankle Explanation |
|----------------------|-------------------------|-------------------------|
| "Dazed" appearance | Cognitive impairment from alcohol | Shock, acute pain, pain medication |
| Watery eyes | Alcohol irritation | Pain response, fatigue (12:49 AM) |
| Unable to provide documents | Confusion from intoxication | Nervousness, stress from police encounter, pain distraction |
| "I have not drank that much" | Defensive minimization | Truthful statement (1-2 drinks below impairment) |
| Unable to follow PBT instructions | Willful refusal or intoxication | Tachypnea from pain, hyperventilation, inability to breathe deeply |
| Unsteady gait (if observed) | Balance impairment from alcohol | **Expected with broken ankle** |
| Slow movements | Depressant effect of alcohol | Protective guarding, pain avoidance |

**Strategic Importance:** At trial, the defense must affirmatively present these alternative explanations through cross-examination and expert testimony. The broken ankle transforms every "clue" of intoxication into an ambiguous indicator.

### C. HGN Evidence at Preliminary Hearing: *Commonwealth v. Weaver*

#### 1. The Two-Tiered Admissibility Standard

Pennsylvania law treats HGN evidence differently depending on the proceeding:

**At Preliminary/Suppression Hearings:**
- Lower evidentiary standard
- Purpose: Establish probable cause, not proof beyond reasonable doubt
- HGN admissible without expert testimony (*Weaver*)

**At Trial:**
- Strict evidentiary standard
- Purpose: Prove guilt beyond reasonable doubt
- HGN inadmissible without expert foundation (*Stringer*)

#### 2. *Commonwealth v. Weaver* - Probable Cause Stage

**Case:** *Commonwealth v. Weaver*, 76 A.3d 562 (Pa. Super. 2013)

**Issue:** Can HGN results alone provide probable cause for arrest without expert testimony?

**Holding:** Yes. HGN results are admissible at preliminary hearings and suppression hearings to establish probable cause, even without the expert foundation required at trial.

**Reasoning:**
1. Probable cause requires only a "reasonable belief" that a crime occurred
2. This is substantially lower than "proof beyond reasonable doubt"
3. Officer's training in HGN administration qualifies them to testify about observations
4. The magistrate may consider the officer's testimony that defendant "failed" HGN
5. The strict scientific foundation required by *Stringer* applies only at trial

**Application to Ulmer:**

At the preliminary hearing, the Commonwealth WILL be permitted to introduce:
- Officer's testimony that he administered the HGN test
- Officer's observations of "lack of smooth pursuit" or "distinct nystagmus"
- Officer's conclusion that defendant "failed" the test

Combined with odor of alcohol and driving violations, this will likely survive the preliminary hearing.

#### 3. Attacking HGN at the Preliminary Stage

While *Weaver* allows the evidence in, the defense can still challenge its weight:

**Cross-Examination Targets:**

□ **Improper administration:**
- Was stimulus held 12-15 inches from nose?
- Was stimulus moved too quickly? (Creates false positives)
- Was stimulus held at maximum deviation for required 4 seconds?
- Did officer check for equal pupil size first?
- Was test conducted in proper lighting?
- Did officer account for eye abnormalities?

□ **Environmental factors:**
- Flashing police lights can affect eye tracking
- Late night (natural nystagmus from fatigue)
- Stress and pain from broken ankle

□ **Alternative causes of nystagmus:**
- Certain medications (pain medication for ankle?)
- Fatigue (12:49 AM)
- Caffeine (in Tito and Soda?)
- Inner ear problems
- Neurological conditions

**Strategic Goal:** Plant seeds of doubt about HGN reliability while preserving *Stringer* objection for trial.

### D. Odor of Alcohol: Insufficient Alone

Pennsylvania courts consistently hold that the odor of alcohol, standing alone, is insufficient to establish probable cause for DUI arrest.

**Reasoning:**
- Odor proves only that alcohol was consumed
- Does not indicate the amount consumed
- Does not prove impairment
- Many people consume alcohol without becoming impaired

**Cases:**
- *Commonwealth v. Theis* (Lycoming C.P. 2015): "The mere odor of alcohol... is not sufficient probable cause to arrest."
- *Commonwealth v. Segida*, 985 A.2d 871 (Pa. 2009): Probable cause requires "totality of circumstances," not single factor

**Application to Ulmer:**

If HGN is successfully attacked and driving violations are de minimis, the Commonwealth's probable cause reduces to:
- Odor of alcohol (insufficient alone)
- Watery eyes and "dazed" appearance (explainable by broken ankle)
- Admission to drinking (not admission to impairment)

This may be insufficient for probable cause, creating suppression opportunity.

## PART IV: THE "CATCH AND RELEASE" PARADOX

This defense theory exploits the logical inconsistency between the Commonwealth's allegation that Ms. Ulmer was "incapable of safe driving" and the arresting officer's contemporaneous decision to release her under Pa.R.Crim.P. 519(B).

### A. Rule 519(B) - Mandatory Release Provisions

**Pennsylvania Rule of Criminal Procedure 519(B)(1):**

> "When an arrest is made without a warrant, the police officer shall... **promptly release** the defendant **unless**:
> 
> (1) the offense is one for which a defendant may be sentenced to imprisonment for more than five years;
> 
> (2) the defendant has one or more prior convictions for a crime of violence;
> 
> (3) **the defendant poses a threat of immediate physical harm to any other person or to the defendant**; or
> 
> (4) the arresting officer has reasonable grounds to believe that the defendant will not appear as required."

**Application to DUI Cases:**

For DUI charges under § 3802(a)(1) (General Impairment, misdemeanor):
- Maximum sentence is 6 months for first offense (not more than 5 years) ✓
- Typically no prior crimes of violence ✓
- Release is **MANDATORY** unless defendant poses threat or is flight risk

### B. The Logical Contradiction

**The Commonwealth's Position:**
- Ms. Ulmer was "rendered incapable of safely driving" under § 3802(a)(1)
- She was so intoxicated that operating a vehicle was unsafe
- Her impairment was severe enough to justify criminal prosecution

**The Officer's Contemporaneous Determination:**
- Released Ms. Ulmer under Rule 519(B)
- Implicit finding: She posed "no threat of immediate physical harm"
- This determination was made within 1-2 hours of arrest
- Likely released to family member or on own recognizance

**The Defense Argument:**

"Officer Miller, you testified that my client was so severely intoxicated that she was 'incapable of safe driving' — that's the legal standard for § 3802(a)(1). You claimed she was staggering, dazed, had watery eyes, and couldn't follow simple instructions.

Yet within an hour of making that determination, you released her under Rule 519 because you found she posed **no threat of immediate physical harm to herself or others**.

Which is it, Officer? 

If she was truly 'incapable' and severely impaired as you claim, she would be a danger to herself simply walking down the street, crossing a road, or existing in an impaired state. A severely intoxicated person poses an inherent threat of harm to themselves.

Your decision to release her proves that her actual impairment was minimal or non-existent. What you observed — the 'dazed' appearance, the unsteady movement — was the result of her broken ankle, not alcohol. That's why you felt comfortable releasing her: because you knew she wasn't actually dangerously impaired."

### C. The "Responsible Party" Counter-Argument

**Commonwealth's Response:**

"The officer released the defendant to her parents [or other sober party]. The release was safe because a responsible, sober adult took custody, not because the defendant was unimpaired."

**Defense Rebuttal:**

1. **Rule 519 Test is Defendant's Condition, Not Custodian's:**
   - The rule asks whether "the defendant poses a threat"
   - It does not ask whether a custodian can mitigate the threat
   - The officer must assess the defendant's dangerousness, not the availability of babysitters

2. **Severely Impaired = Dangerous Even With Custodian:**
   - If someone is "incapable of safe driving" due to severe intoxication, they remain dangerous
   - They could stumble and fall, wander away, become belligerent, harm themselves
   - A truly severely impaired person should not be released even to family

3. **The Officer's Actions Reveal His True Belief:**
   - If the officer genuinely believed the impairment was severe, he would have:
     - Taken her to hospital for medical clearance
     - Held her until sober
     - Expressed concerns to the custodian about her level of impairment
   - The prompt, routine release indicates the officer believed the impairment was minimal

4. **Alternative Explanation:**
   - The officer released her because what he observed was primarily attributable to the broken ankle
   - The "dazed" appearance, slow movements, and difficulty were pain-related
   - The officer recognized this at some level, hence the comfort with release

### D. Practical Application in Cross-Examination

**Sample Cross-Examination:**

Q: Officer, you released Ms. Ulmer under Rule 519, correct?  
A: Yes.

Q: Rule 519 requires you to release a defendant unless they pose a threat of immediate physical harm, correct?  
A: Yes.

Q: So when you released Ms. Ulmer, you determined she posed no threat of immediate physical harm to herself, correct?  
A: She was released to her mother.

Q: I didn't ask about her mother. I asked about your determination. You determined she posed no threat of immediate physical harm, yes or no?  
A: Given that her mother was taking her, no threat.

Q: If Ms. Ulmer was as severely intoxicated as you testified—staggering, dazed, unable to follow instructions—wouldn't she pose a danger to herself even in her mother's care?  
A: She wasn't driving.

Q: But a severely intoxicated person is dangerous in general, correct? They could fall, injure themselves, wander into traffic?  
A: I suppose.

Q: Yet you felt comfortable releasing her. That's because the behaviors you observed—the dazed appearance, the difficulty moving—were primarily due to her broken ankle and the pain she was experiencing, not severe alcohol intoxication, correct?  
A: I believed she had been drinking.

Q: That's not what I asked. The reason you felt comfortable releasing her is because you knew the physical signs were primarily from the injury, not alcohol. Isn't that true?

### E. Strategic Value

The "Catch and Release" argument is particularly powerful because:

1. **It Uses the Officer's Own Actions Against Him:**
   - Courts trust officer's contemporaneous judgments
   - His release decision contradicts his impairment testimony

2. **It Highlights the Role of the Broken Ankle:**
   - Forces jury to reconsider every "clue" through the lens of injury
   - Makes the medical explanation more credible

3. **It Creates Reasonable Doubt:**
   - Even if jury believes some impairment existed, the contradiction raises doubt about severity
   - § 3802(a)(1) requires proof of being "rendered incapable" — a high standard
   - If officer didn't believe it was that severe (evidenced by release), why should jury?

## PART V: THE 5-DAY RULE AND PROCEDURAL VIOLATIONS

### A. Rule 519(B)(2) - The 5-Day Filing Requirement

**Pennsylvania Rule of Criminal Procedure 519(B)(2):**

> "Whenever a defendant is released pursuant to paragraph (B)(1), a **criminal complaint shall be filed against the defendant within 5 days** of the defendant's release."

**Purpose:**
- Ensures prompt filing of charges
- Protects defendant's ability to secure evidence
- Provides notice of charges while events are fresh

### B. Timeline Analysis: Rule Violation in Ulmer

**Critical Dates:**
- **October 26, 2025:** Arrest and release
- **November 4, 2025:** Criminal complaint filed
- **Gap:** **9 days** (exceeds the 5-day requirement by 4 days)

**Violation:** The Commonwealth failed to comply with Rule 519(B)(2).

### C. Legal Consequences: The *Revtai* Framework

**Case:** *Commonwealth v. Revtai*

**Issue:** What happens when the Commonwealth violates the 5-day rule?

**Holding:** Violation of the 5-day rule does not result in automatic dismissal. However, it triggers a prejudice analysis under Pa.R.Crim.P. 109.

**Framework:**
1. The defendant must demonstrate the delay occurred
2. The defendant must show actual prejudice resulted from the delay
3. The burden is on the defendant to establish prejudice
4. If prejudice is shown, the remedy may be dismissal or suppression of evidence

### D. Demonstrating Actual Prejudice

#### 1. Loss of Surveillance Footage

**Argument:**

The stop occurred at a **closed Walgreens parking lot** at approximately 12:49 AM. Commercial establishments typically maintain surveillance systems with the following characteristics:

- **Recording duration:** 7-14 days before overwriting
- **Coverage:** Parking lots, entrances, and surrounding areas
- **Quality:** High-definition, time-stamped footage

**Potential Exculpatory Value:**

The Walgreens surveillance footage could have captured:
- Ms. Ulmer's driving pattern entering the parking lot (smooth vs. erratic)
- Her gait and movement exiting the vehicle (consistent with broken ankle vs. intoxication)
- Her performance on the HGN test (verifiable administration, defendant's focus and tracking)
- Her attempt to provide a breath sample (genuine effort vs. refusal)
- Overall demeanor and behavior (injury-related distress vs. intoxication)

**The Prejudice:**

By delaying the filing until day 9, the Commonwealth effectively denied Ms. Ulmer timely notice of the charges. By the time she learned of the prosecution (November 4), the surveillance footage had likely been overwritten (typical 7-day retention). She was unable to subpoena this potentially exculpatory evidence.

**Legal Standard:**

Under *Commonwealth v. Williams* and related cases, the defendant need not prove the evidence would have been exculpatory, only that:
1. The evidence potentially existed
2. It could have been exculpatory
3. The delay caused its loss
4. The defendant had no earlier opportunity to preserve it

#### 2. Medical Documentation Prejudice

**Argument:**

The broken ankle is the linchpin of the defense. Immediate medical documentation was critical:

**What Could Have Been Documented:**
- X-rays showing fracture severity
- Emergency room notes from the date of arrest
- Documentation of pain medication administered
- Physician assessment of mobility limitations
- Contemporaneous photographs of swelling, bruising, cast/boot

**The Prejudice:**

The 9-day delay meant Ms. Ulmer did not know she needed to urgently document her injury with medical providers. By November 4:
- Swelling and bruising had diminished
- The injury had partially healed
- Pain levels had decreased
- The opportunity for contemporaneous medical documentation was lost

**Impact:**

At trial, the Commonwealth will argue, "How do we know the ankle was actually broken on October 26? Where's the proof?" The delay prejudiced her ability to provide that immediate medical corroboration.

#### 3. Witness Memory Degradation

**Argument:**

Any witnesses to Ms. Ulmer's condition on October 26 (family members, friends who saw her that day, anyone at the Walgreens area) would have fresher, more reliable memories if interviewed within 5 days versus 9+ days later.

The delay degraded the quality of witness testimony available to the defense.

### E. Strategic Use of 5-Day Rule Violation

**Motion to Dismiss or Suppress:**

While dismissal is unlikely, the defense should file a motion to:
1. Highlight the violation
2. Document the prejudice
3. Request sanctions (even if denied, creates appellate issue)
4. Seek adverse inference instruction to jury regarding lost surveillance footage

**Jury Argument:**

"Ladies and gentlemen, the Commonwealth had 5 days to file charges. They waited 9 days. Why? During those extra 4 days, the Walgreens surveillance footage—which would have shown you Ms. Ulmer's actual condition, her actual movements, her actual behavior—was erased. Gone forever. The Commonwealth's delay destroyed evidence that could have proven her innocence. When evidence disappears due to the government's failure to follow its own rules, you must view their case with suspicion."

## PART VI: THE "PHANTOM REFUSAL" DEFENSE

This sophisticated defense theory challenges the validity of the recorded chemical test refusal by arguing Ms. Ulmer was not provided a "meaningful opportunity" to submit to testing and that her inability to complete the test was medically caused, not willful.

### A. Pennsylvania's Implied Consent Law (75 Pa.C.S. § 1547)

#### 1. Statutory Framework

**§ 1547(a) - Implied Consent:**

> "Any person who drives, operates or is in actual physical control of the movement of a vehicle in this Commonwealth shall be deemed to have given consent... to one or more chemical tests of breath... for the purpose of determining the alcoholic content of blood..."

**§ 1547(b) - Refusal Consequences:**

> "If any person... refuses to submit to chemical testing, the testing shall not be conducted but upon notice by the police officer, the department shall suspend the operating privilege of the person..."

**§ 1547(c) - Test Requirements:**

> "Chemical testing shall be administered at the request of a police officer having reasonable grounds to believe the person to have been driving... while under the influence of alcohol..."

#### 2. The DL-26B Warnings

Before requesting a chemical test, the officer must read the DL-26B form, which includes:

1. You are under arrest for driving under the influence
2. I am requesting you submit to a chemical test (breath or blood)
3. If you refuse, your license will be suspended for at least 12 months
4. If you refuse and are convicted, you face increased penalties
5. If you refuse, your refusal may be used against you in court

**According to Affidavit:** Officer Miller read the DL-26B form verbatim at approximately 01:04 hours.

### B. The "Meaningful Opportunity" Standard

#### 1. *Solomon v. Department of Transportation*

**Case:** *Solomon v. DOT*, 974 A.2d 1064 (Pa. Cmwlth. 2009)

**Issue:** What constitutes a valid refusal under Pennsylvania's implied consent law?

**Holding:** For a refusal to be legally valid, the motorist must be provided with a **"meaningful opportunity"** to submit to the test.

**Key Principles:**

1. **More Than Just Reading Warnings:**
   - Simply reading the DL-26B form is necessary but not sufficient
   - The officer must ensure the environment and circumstances allow for a voluntary, informed decision

2. **Ambiguous Conduct:**
   - Questions, hesitation, or momentary silence are not per se refusals
   - Officer must allow reasonable time for the person to process information and decide

3. **Termination of Opportunity:**
   - If an officer interprets ambiguous conduct as refusal and immediately terminates the process, the "opportunity" was illusory
   - Example: Officer asks, "Will you take the test?"; defendant says, "Can I call my lawyer first?"; officer responds, "That's a refusal" and ends process

**Application to Ulmer:**

According to the affidavit:
- PBT was attempted first (defendant "unable to follow instructions")
- Later, at approximately 01:04, DL-26B was read and defendant "refused"

**Critical Questions:**
- What exactly did Ms. Ulmer say or do that constituted "refusal"?
- Did she explicitly say "I refuse"?
- Or did she attempt to blow but fail due to pain/inability?
- Did the officer interpret her difficulty as refusal?

#### 2. The "Roadside Refusal" Problem

**Issue:** Where was the refusal recorded?

**Two Scenarios:**

**Scenario A - Roadside Refusal:**
- Officer read DL-26B at the scene or in patrol car
- Defendant refused at roadside
- Was never transported to station/hospital for actual testing

**Scenario B - Facility Refusal:**
- Defendant transported to PSP barracks or hospital
- DL-26B read at facility
- Defendant refused at testing location

**Why It Matters:**

A roadside refusal is procedurally problematic:

1. **No Immediate Test Available:**
   - The Intoxilyzer machines are at police stations, not patrol cars
   - Blood draws require medical facility
   - At roadside, the "threat" of the test is abstract

2. **Environmental Factors:**
   - Chaotic scene (flashing lights, traffic, darkness)
   - Physical discomfort (handcuffs, back of patrol car)
   - Pain from broken ankle
   - Emotional stress

3. **Incomplete Opportunity:**
   - A defendant might waffle or express reluctance at roadside
   - But upon arriving at a controlled environment (station), they might reconsider
   - By accepting a roadside refusal and not transporting, the officer denies the defendant the full window of opportunity

**Defense Argument:**

"The law says a defendant has the right to refuse chemical testing. But it also requires that they be given a **meaningful opportunity** to consent. That opportunity cannot be meaningful if it's given in the back of a police car, in the dark, while my client is in severe pain from a broken ankle, and when no testing equipment is even present.

Officer Miller should have transported Ms. Ulmer to the barracks, allowed her to sit in a climate-controlled room, explained the test procedure with the Intoxilyzer in front of her, and THEN asked for her decision. By accepting what he claims was a 'refusal' at the roadside, he denied her the meaningful opportunity the law requires."

### C. The Legal Definition of Refusal: *Commonwealth v. Renwick*

#### 1. The "Unqualified Assent" Standard

**Case:** *Commonwealth v. Renwick*, 669 A.2d 934 (Pa. Cmwlth. 1995)

**Holding:** Under Pennsylvania law, **anything less than an unqualified, unequivocal assent to take the test constitutes a refusal**.

**Examples of "Refusals" Under *Renwick*:**
- Explicit refusal: "No, I won't take the test"
- Conditional consent: "I'll take it after I call my lawyer"
- Equivocation: "I'm not sure" or "I don't know"
- Silence or non-response
- **Physical inability to provide sufficient sample**

**The Harsh Reality:**

The *Renwick* standard is strict. Even a defendant who wants to take the test but asks reasonable questions or hesitates can be deemed to have refused.

#### 2. The "Insufficient Sample" Refusal

**Issue:** What if the defendant attempts to blow but the machine does not register a sufficient sample?

**Rule:** This is treated as a refusal.

**Commonwealth's Position:**
- The Intoxilyzer printout will show "insufficient sample" or "test not completed"
- This is prima facie evidence of refusal
- The Commonwealth need not prove the defendant was faking or deliberately failing
- The machine's failure to obtain a sample is enough

**Application to Ulmer:**

The affidavit states Ms. Ulmer was "unable to follow instructions for blowing into the PBT."

This language suggests one of two scenarios:
1. She made no attempt (explicit refusal)
2. She attempted but failed to provide adequate sample (insufficient sample refusal)

If scenario #2, the refusal is vulnerable to the "medical inability" defense.

### D. The "Medical Inability" Affirmative Defense

#### 1. The Burden-Shifting Framework

**Case:** *Department of Transportation v. Gross*, 605 A.2d 433 (Pa. Cmwlth. 1992)

**Framework:**

**Step 1 - Commonwealth's Burden:**
- Commonwealth must prove defendant refused testing
- Proven by: DL-26B warnings given + refusal recorded + insufficient sample printout
- Burden of proof: Preponderance of evidence

**Step 2 - Burden Shifts to Defendant:**
- Once refusal established, burden shifts entirely to defendant
- Defendant must prove they were **physically unable** to take the test
- Burden of proof: Preponderance of evidence
- **Critical:** Defendant's testimony alone is insufficient

**Step 3 - Requirement for Expert Medical Testimony:**
- Defendant must present competent medical evidence
- Expert must establish causal link between medical condition and inability to blow
- Expert must testify the inability was genuine, not volitional

#### 2. Building the Medical Inability Defense

**The Physiological Argument:**

**Medical Expert Must Testify:**

**A. The Injury:**
- Ms. Ulmer suffered an acute ankle fracture
- Ankle fractures cause severe, acute pain
- Pain is rated 7-10/10 on pain scale

**B. The Physiological Response:**
- Acute pain triggers the sympathetic nervous system ("fight or flight")
- This causes:
  - Tachypnea (rapid, shallow breathing)
  - Hyperventilation
  - Increased heart rate
  - Elevated blood pressure
  - Release of stress hormones (cortisol, adrenaline)

**C. Respiratory Mechanics:**
- Normal breathing: 12-16 breaths per minute, moderate depth
- Tachypnea: 20-30+ breaths per minute, shallow depth
- The Intoxilyzer 8000/9000 requires:
  - Deep inhalation
  - Sustained exhalation for 6-8 seconds
  - Consistent pressure of 1.5+ liters
  - Alveolar air (deep lung air, not mouth air)

**D. The Incompatibility:**
- A person in acute pain with tachypnea is physiologically incapable of:
  - Taking a deep breath (pain prevents full chest expansion)
  - Sustaining exhalation for 6-8 seconds (rapid breathing pattern interferes)
  - Maintaining consistent pressure (panic/pain response disrupts control)
  - Producing alveolar air (shallow breathing doesn't reach deep lungs)

**E. The Conclusion:**
- Ms. Ulmer's inability to provide a breath sample was not volitional
- It was an involuntary physiological response to acute pain
- No amount of willpower could overcome this medical limitation

#### 3. Expert Witness Requirements

**Who Can Testify:**
- Emergency medicine physician
- Pulmonologist
- Anesthesiologist
- Certified pain management specialist

**What They Must Address:**
1. The nature and severity of ankle fractures
2. The pain response and sympathetic activation
3. Respiratory physiology and tachypnea
4. The requirements of the Intoxilyzer machine
5. Why the two are incompatible
6. Why this was involuntary, not a choice

**Documents Needed:**
- Medical records from October 26-27, 2025
- X-rays showing fracture
- Emergency room notes (if she sought treatment)
- Photographs of injury
- Intoxilyzer technical manual (to establish breath requirements)

#### 4. Strategic Impact

**If Successful:**

The medical inability defense accomplishes three critical goals:

1. **Negates License Suspension:**
   - At the PennDOT administrative hearing, if medical inability is proven, the suspension is lifted
   - Defendant keeps driving privileges

2. **Neutralizes Consciousness of Guilt:**
   - At trial, the Commonwealth will argue: "She refused because she knew she was drunk"
   - Defense counters: "She didn't refuse; she was physically unable due to injury"
   - This removes the damaging inference

3. **Reinforces Broken Ankle Theme:**
   - Everything comes back to the injury
   - Jury sees consistent defense theory
   - "Every single thing the officer attributed to intoxication was actually the injury"

## PART VII: MATERIAL OMISSIONS IN THE AFFIDAVIT - THE *FRANKS* DOCTRINE

This advanced defense strategy attacks the constitutional validity of the arrest by arguing the officer's affidavit contained material omissions that, if included, would have negated probable cause.

### A. The *Franks v. Delaware* Standard

**Case:** *Franks v. Delaware*, 438 U.S. 154 (1978)

**Holding:** A defendant may challenge the veracity of statements in an affidavit used to obtain a warrant (or establish probable cause for warrantless arrest). If the defendant makes a substantial preliminary showing that:

1. The affiant made a **false statement** or omitted a **material fact**,
2. **Knowingly, intentionally, or with reckless disregard for the truth**, and
3. The false statement or omission was **necessary to the finding of probable cause**,

then the defendant is entitled to a hearing. If the allegation is proven, the warrant is voided and evidence suppressed.

**Pennsylvania Application:**

Pennsylvania courts have extended *Franks* to warrantless arrests. An affidavit of probable cause supporting a warrantless arrest must be truthful and complete.

**Key Case:** *Commonwealth v. Milliken* and related Pennsylvania cases recognize that material omissions—not just affirmative false statements—can invalidate probable cause.

### B. The Material Omission: The Broken Ankle

#### 1. What the Affidavit States

**From Officer Miller's Affidavit:**

✓ Strong odor of alcoholic beverage  
✓ Appeared "dazed"  
✓ Watery eyes  
✓ Unable to provide correct documents  
✓ Admitted drinking  
✓ Only HGN test conducted "due to ULMER informing me she had a broken ankle"  
✓ Unable to follow PBT instructions  

**What the Affidavit OMITS:**

✗ Detailed description of the broken ankle (was it visibly swollen? In a cast? A boot?)  
✗ How the broken ankle affected her mobility and demeanor  
✗ That "dazed" appearance and difficulty moving are expected with acute fracture  
✗ That pain from the fracture provides an innocent explanation for her presentation  
✗ That the inability to perform FSTs and PBT may be pain-related, not intoxication-related  

#### 2. The Reckless Omission Argument

**Defense Position:**

Officer Miller's affidavit was recklessly incomplete. While he mentioned the broken ankle in passing (to explain why he didn't do WAT/OLS), he failed to include material information about how the injury affected every observation he attributed to intoxication.

**The Omission Was Reckless Because:**

1. **Officer Knew the Injury Was Significant:**
   - Significant enough to preclude two of three standard field sobriety tests
   - Ms. Ulmer specifically informed him of the injury
   - He observed her condition firsthand

2. **Officer Should Have Known It Was Material:**
   - A broken ankle causes pain, distress, difficulty moving
   - These symptoms mimic intoxication
   - A reasonable officer would recognize this overlap
   - By omitting this context, he misled the magistrate

3. **The Omission Was Material:**
   - If the magistrate knew "dazed appearance" and "unsteady gait" were likely pain-related, probable cause weakens
   - If the magistrate knew "unable to follow PBT instructions" might be respiratory distress from pain, the refusal evidence weakens
   - The omission directly impacted the probable cause determination

### C. Reconstructing the Affidavit with Full Disclosure

**Current Affidavit Language:**

> "I immediately detected a strong odor of an alcoholic beverage emanating from her person. I also observed ULMER to appear dazed and have watery eyes."

**How It Should Have Read:**

> "I immediately detected a strong odor of an alcoholic beverage emanating from her person. I also observed ULMER to appear dazed and have watery eyes. **However, ULMER reported she had a broken ankle and appeared to be in significant pain. I observed [cast/boot/swelling]. The dazed appearance and watery eyes could be attributable to acute pain from the fracture.** I also note that the time was approximately 01:00 hours, and watery eyes may result from fatigue."

**Current Affidavit Language:**

> "ULMER was unable to follow instructions for blowing into the PBT."

**How It Should Have Read:**

> "ULMER was unable to follow instructions for blowing into the PBT. **ULMER appeared to be attempting to blow but could not produce a sufficient sample. This may have been due to difficulty breathing deeply because of pain from her broken ankle, which can cause rapid, shallow breathing (tachypnea).** I therefore cannot conclusively determine whether this was a willful refusal or a medical inability."

**Current Affidavit Language:**

> "The only SFST conducted was the Horizontal Gaze Nystagmus test (HGN), due to ULMER informing me she had a broken ankle."

**How It Should Have Read:**

> "The only SFST conducted was the Horizontal Gaze Nystagmus test (HGN), due to ULMER informing me she had a broken ankle. **The Walk-and-Turn and One-Leg Stand tests were medically contraindicated because ULMER could not safely bear weight on her injured ankle. The inability to conduct these tests significantly limits my ability to assess impairment through standardized methods. Any unsteady gait or balance issues I observed could be equally attributable to the ankle injury rather than alcohol consumption.**"

### D. The Probable Cause Analysis

**Magistrate's Probable Cause Determination (Based on Actual Affidavit):**

- Odor of alcohol ✓
- Dazed appearance ✓
- Watery eyes ✓
- Difficulty providing documents ✓
- Admitted drinking ✓
- Failed/couldn't complete FSTs ✓

**Conclusion:** Probable cause exists.

**Magistrate's Probable Cause Determination (With Complete Information):**

- Odor of alcohol ✓ (but proves consumption, not impairment)
- Dazed appearance ⚠️ (likely pain from broken ankle)
- Watery eyes ⚠️ (likely pain or fatigue at 1 AM)
- Difficulty providing documents ⚠️ (stress, pain, nervousness)
- Admitted drinking ✓ (but not amount or impairment level)
- Failed/couldn't complete FSTs ❌ (medically invalid due to injury; no evidentiary value)
- Refused/unable PBT ⚠️ (possibly medical inability, not willful refusal)

**Conclusion:** Without the FST results (invalid) and with alternative explanations for all observations, probable cause is questionable. The totality of circumstances does not clearly establish impairment vs. injury + minimal drinking.

### E. The *Franks* Motion Strategy

**Motion for Franks Hearing**

**Relief Requested:**
1. Evidentiary hearing to examine the omission
2. If omission proven reckless and material, suppression of all evidence
3. Dismissal of charges as fruit of poisonous tree

**Burden:**

- If successful, hearing is granted
- At hearing, defendant must prove omission was reckless and material by preponderance

**Evidence to Present:**

**Likelihood of Success:**

Difficult but not impossible. Pennsylvania courts are generally deferential to officer affidavits. However, where the omission is glaring and the alternative explanation is strong, courts have granted relief.

**Strategic Value Even If Denied:**

## PART VIII: HGN EVIDENCE AT TRIAL - THE *STRINGER* EXCLUSION

If the case proceeds to trial, the evidentiary standard shifts dramatically. The permissive *Weaver* standard (allowing HGN at prelim) is replaced by the strict *Stringer* requirement.

### A. *Commonwealth v. Stringer* - The Foundational Case

**Case:** *Commonwealth v. Stringer*, 678 A.2d 1200 (Pa. Super. 1996)

**Issue:** Is HGN admissible at trial without expert testimony establishing scientific foundation?

**Holding:** No. HGN is scientific evidence requiring expert foundation under the *Frye* test (now Pa.R.E. 702).

**Reasoning:**

1. **HGN is Not Common Knowledge:**
   - The correlation between nystagmus and blood alcohol is not within the common understanding of jurors
   - Unlike observations of slurred speech or staggering, nystagmus requires specialized knowledge

2. **Officer is Not an Expert:**
   - Officer may be trained to administer the test
   - But officer cannot explain the ophthalmological science underlying it
   - Officer cannot testify about why nystagmus occurs or its reliability

3. **Foundation Required:**
   - Commonwealth must present an expert witness (ophthalmologist, optometrist, or highly qualified DRE)
   - Expert must establish:
     - HGN testing is generally accepted in the relevant scientific community
     - The test is reliable for detecting alcohol impairment
     - The specific administration in this case complied with protocols

4. **Without Foundation:**
   - HGN evidence is inadmissible
   - Arresting officer cannot testify about HGN results

### B. The Two-Tiered Admissibility Standard

| Stage | Case | Standard | HGN Admissible? | Foundation Required? |
|-------|------|----------|-----------------|---------------------|
| **Preliminary Hearing** | *Weaver* | Probable cause (reasonable belief) | ✓ YES | ❌ NO - Officer testimony sufficient |
| **Suppression Hearing** | *Weaver* | Probable cause (reasonable belief) | ✓ YES | ❌ NO - Officer testimony sufficient |
| **Trial** | *Stringer* | Proof beyond reasonable doubt | ❌ NO* | ✓ YES - Expert testimony required |

*Unless Commonwealth provides proper expert foundation

### C. The Commonwealth's Dilemma at Trial

**Option 1: Call an Expert Witness**

**Who Qualifies:**
- Ophthalmologist
- Optometrist
- Senior DRE (Drug Recognition Expert) instructor with advanced credentials
- Possibly a forensic toxicologist with ophthalmology background

**What It Costs:**
- Expert fees: $3,000-$10,000+
- Court time: Full day of trial
- Preparation time: Hours of attorney consultation

**Practical Reality:**

For a **misdemeanor** General Impairment case (§ 3802(a)(1)), District Attorney's Offices rarely allocate resources for expert witnesses. The cost-benefit analysis doesn't support it.

**Typical DA Response:**
- Proceed without HGN evidence
- Rely on other evidence (driving, observations, refusal)
- Offer plea deal to avoid trial

**Option 2: Proceed Without HGN Evidence**

**What Remains:**
- Driving pattern (high beams, lane straddling) - potentially weak if de minimis
- Physical observations (odor, eyes, demeanor) - explainable by broken ankle
- Statements ("I have not drank that much") - ambiguous
- Refusal - powerful, but neutralized if medical inability proven

**Result:** Significantly weaker case. Reasonable doubt becomes likely.

### D. Motion in Limine to Exclude HGN

**Motion in Limine: Exclude HGN Evidence Absent Expert Testimony**

**Legal Standard:**
- *Commonwealth v. Stringer*, 678 A.2d 1200 (Pa. Super. 1996)
- Pa.R.E. 702 (expert testimony requirements)

**Argument:**

1. HGN is scientific evidence requiring foundation
2. Arresting officer is not qualified to provide foundation
3. Commonwealth has not noticed any expert witnesses
4. Without expert, HGN results are inadmissible
5. Any reference to HGN in officer's testimony would be prejudicial

**Requested Relief:**
- Preclude any testimony about HGN testing
- Preclude any reference to "eye test" or observations of nystagmus
- If Commonwealth intends to offer HGN, require advance disclosure of expert and *Frye* hearing

**Likelihood:** **High.** Pennsylvania courts consistently apply *Stringer* at trial stage.

### E. Strategic Impact of HGN Exclusion

**The Evidentiary Cascade:**

1. **WAT and OLS:** ❌ Excluded (medically invalid due to broken ankle)
2. **HGN:** ❌ Excluded (no expert foundation per *Stringer*)
3. **PBT:** ❌ Inadmissible in Pennsylvania (investigatory tool only)
4. **Chemical Test BAC:** ❌ No result (refusal)

**What's Left:**
- Driving observations (weak if de minimis)
- Odor of alcohol (insufficient alone)
- Physical appearance (explainable by injury)
- Refusal (neutralized by medical inability)

**Conclusion:** Without field sobriety tests and without BAC, the Commonwealth's case for "incapable of safe driving" becomes largely subjective and circumstantial.

## PART IX: THE CHEMICAL TEST REFUSAL AT TRIAL

### A. Admissibility: *Commonwealth v. Bell*

**Case:** *Commonwealth v. Bell*, 211 A.3d 761 (Pa. 2019)

**Issue:** Is evidence of a defendant's refusal to submit to chemical testing admissible at a DUI trial?

**Holding:** Yes. Evidence of refusal is admissible to show "consciousness of guilt."

**Reasoning:**

1. **Probative Value:**
   - A person who refuses testing may do so because they know they are impaired
   - The refusal is circumstantial evidence of guilt
   - Jury may infer the defendant refused because testing would have confirmed intoxication

2. **Not Testimonial:**
   - Refusal is conduct, not compelled testimony
   - Fifth Amendment does not bar its admission

3. **Balancing Test:**
   - Probative value outweighs prejudicial effect
   - Defendant can offer alternative explanations for refusal

**Application:** The Commonwealth WILL introduce evidence that Ms. Ulmer refused testing, and will argue this shows consciousness of guilt.

### B. Neutralizing the Refusal Through Medical Inability

**The Defense Counter-Narrative:**

**Standard Commonwealth Argument:**
> "Ladies and gentlemen, Anne Ulmer refused to take the breath test. Why? Because she knew she was drunk. She knew that machine would show she was over the limit, so she refused. Her refusal is evidence of her guilt."

**Defense Rebuttal with Medical Inability:**

> "The Commonwealth wants you to believe Ms. Ulmer refused the breath test because she was hiding something. But the evidence shows something very different.
> 
> Ms. Ulmer had a broken ankle. Dr. [Expert] testified that a broken ankle causes severe pain, which triggers a physiological stress response. Your body goes into fight-or-flight mode. Your breathing becomes rapid and shallow—it's called tachypnea.
> 
> The Intoxilyzer machine requires you to take a deep breath and blow for 6-8 seconds in a sustained, controlled manner. Dr. [Expert] explained that someone in acute pain with rapid, shallow breathing simply cannot do that. It's not a choice. It's not willful. It's a medical reality.
> 
> Ms. Ulmer didn't refuse because she was hiding intoxication. She couldn't complete the test because she was in agony from a broken bone. The Commonwealth wants to turn her injury—a medical condition—into evidence of guilt. That's not justice."

**Impact:**

If the medical inability defense is properly established through expert testimony, it:
- Removes the "consciousness of guilt" inference
- Transforms the refusal from damaging to neutral or even helpful
- Reinforces the central defense theme: Everything attributed to intoxication was actually the injury

### C. Jury Instructions on Refusal

**Standard Instruction:**

"You have heard evidence that the defendant refused to submit to chemical testing. You may consider this evidence along with all other evidence in determining whether the defendant was driving under the influence. However, you are not required to draw any inference from the refusal. The defendant's refusal to submit to testing is not proof of guilt by itself."

**Defense-Requested Modification:**

"You have heard evidence regarding the defendant's inability to complete breath testing. The defendant has presented evidence that this inability was due
